• Draw on Tolstoy’s existential despair and Nietzsche’s proclamation of the “death of God.”

• Nihilism as a profound disruption of one’s sense of self and purpose.

• Types of Nihilism: Within Nihiltheism: Worldly & Transcendent experiences of Nihilism

• The First Call:

## Chapter 2: The Worldly Nihilistic Experience


WW2.1 The Absurd Perspective

• Confronting the Void:

• How the experience of worldly nihilism culminates in the Absurd Perspective.

• Reference Emil Cioran’s _On the Heights of Despair_: _“Man starts over again every day, in spite of overwhelming evidence that he will fail yet again.”_

• The Role of Anxiety:

• Anxiety as an existential mood that reveals the nullity of all possibilities.

• Kierkegaard: _“Anxiety is the dizziness of freedom.”_


#### 2.2 The Call to the Infinite

• From Within, Yet Beyond:

• Heidegger’s _call_ as both internal and transcendent, drawing one toward the Infinite Presence.

• Augustine: _“Our heart is restless until it rests in you.”_

• Toward the Infinite:

• Discuss how the worldly experience of nihilism prepares the ground for an encounter with the eternal.



## Chapter 3: The Temporal vs. Eternal Struggle


#### 3.1 The Divided Self

• Double-Mindedness:

• The existential conflict between worldly attachments and spiritual aspirations.

• Reference Plato’s disdain for the human body and its pull away from the Eternal Forms.

• Falling Back into the “They”:

• Heidegger’s critique of inauthenticity and the constant temptation to conform to societal norms.


#### 3.2 Longing for the Eternal

• The Struggle of Being:

• William James: _“There are two lives, the natural and the spiritual, and we must lose the one before we can participate in the other.”_

• Augustine’s spiritual turmoil: _“My two wills, one carnal and one spiritual, struggled within me.”_

• The Abyss Between Finite and Infinite:

• Martin Buber: _“Being is split, with one part abandoned to hopelessness.”_

#### 4.1 Nihilism and the Desire for Nonbeing

• Discuss Mitchell Heisman’s 1900-page suicide note as a case study in philosophical suicide.

• Nonexistence as Liberation:

• Explore Schopenhauer’s and Cioran’s perspectives on life as a burden.

• The allure of nonbeing as a rejection of the absurdity of existence.


#### 4.2 The Ethics of Nonexistence

• Moral Implications:

• Address ethical questions surrounding the act of self-destruction in the face of meaninglessness.

• The Role of Suffering:

• Nietzsche’s _“Amor fati”_: Embracing suffering as a necessary condition of existence.
