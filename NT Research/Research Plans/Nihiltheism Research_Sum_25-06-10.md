# Comprehensive Foundations of Nihilism and Nihiltheism

## Nihiltheism and the Core Existential Questions

Building on the comprehensive foundations of nihilism and nihiltheism, we now turn to **five core existential questions** and examine each through a Nihiltheistic lens. These questions cover the nature and limits of knowledge, the basis of values and ethics, the meaning of being vs. nothingness, the human experience of meaninglessness, and the possibility of transcendence amid nihilism. The Nihiltheistic framework – which acknowledges the nihilist’s “nothingness” while seeking an engaged, transcendent response – provides unique insights into each area. Below, each question is addressed with historical context, cross-cultural perspectives, and implications for Nihiltheism, supported by authoritative sources.

## Knowledge and Truth: The Epistemological Question

**Question:***What can we know, if anything, in a world that may lack inherent meaning or truth?* This is the epistemological concern at the heart of nihilism. If all truths and meanings are human constructs or illusions, the nihilist claims that objective knowledge or certainty is impossible. Extreme skepticism has long been linked to nihilism: ancient Skeptics denied the possibility of certainty, seeing all beliefs as unjustified opinions. In modern philosophy, this evolves into **epistemological nihilism**, or **antifoundationalism**, which rejects any firm basis for knowledge or truth. Postmodern thinkers like Lyotard and Derrida exemplify this stance – they show *“incredulity toward metanarratives”* and argue that **“absolutes are merely fictional forms,”** meaning our truths are transient and contingent. In short, from a nihilist perspective, **reason is impotent and “there is simply no true world”** behind our beliefs. Every belief is seen as false at some level because it cannot escape the fundamental doubt that nothing has an objective foundation.

- **Skepticism and Relativism:** Nihilistic thought often begins in radical doubt. If *“what each man wishes to believe, that he believes,”* as one classical skeptic noted, then no claim to truth rises above subjective preference. This yields a relativistic outlook where knowledge is context-bound and uncertain.
- **Postmodern Epistemic Nihilism:** In the 20th century, nihilism’s epistemological thread became associated with postmodern critiques of truth. Lyotard’s notion that grand narratives (universal explanations) no longer hold and Derrida’s idea that we *“can never be sure that what one knows corresponds with what is”* reflect a prevailing sense that **all knowledge is unstable**. Truths are provisional, **“genuine only until something more palatable replaces it”**.
- **“Unknowing” and Mystical Insight:** Nihiltheism takes this acknowledgment of **knowledge’s limits** and turns it into a kind of wisdom. Since nothing can be known with absolute certainty, Nihiltheism aligns with traditions of **“learned ignorance”** – for example, apophatic (negative) theology and mystical philosophies that claim the highest truth is unknowable in rational terms. Nihiltheism **“acknowledges that language… is inherently limited in its ability to fully capture the Transcendent reality”**, urging a symbolic or paradoxical approach to truth. Rather than seeing the failure of knowledge as a dead end, this perspective transforms it into *“unknowing knowing,”* a humble recognition that embracing mystery is itself a form of insight. By **accepting that ultimate reality eludes conceptual grasp**, one remains open to transcendent understanding beyond logical certainty.

In summary, the epistemological stance within Nihiltheism is one of **deep humility about knowledge**. It fully accepts the nihilist critique that no belief has a perfect foundation and that *“epistemological failure”* is part of the human condition. However, instead of despairing, Nihiltheism encourages us to find freedom in this uncertainty – to engage with the mystery. This may involve practices of silence, **“unknowing”** (as in mystical contemplation), or even the use of perspectives outside ordinary rationality (e.g. insights from altered states of consciousness) to glimpse truths that cannot be neatly expressed. In a Nihiltheistic view, *not knowing* is not mere ignorance but an honest starting point for a more profound wisdom, one that sees through illusions of certainty and remains open to whatever reality, or nothingness, may ultimately be.

## Values and Ethics: The Axiological Question

**Question:***How should we live and act if life has no inherent meaning? Are there values or morals after nihilism?* This is the axiological (value-centered) challenge. **Moral or ethical nihilism** traditionally answers that there are **no absolute values** – concepts of good and evil are *“nebulous, the product of nothing more than social and emotive pressures”*. In other words, if no objective meaning exists, then no action can be definitively called “right” or “wrong” in any universal sense. Nietzsche captured this collapse of value by showing that once we recognize all values as human projections, *“the highest values devalue themselves… ‘Why?’ finds no answer”*. Under nihilism’s withering scrutiny, every moral or purpose we held sacred is exposed as contingent or empty, leading to a **“collapse of meaning, relevance, and purpose”**. The result can be a dangerous void: if *“ultimately Nothingness prevails and the world is meaningless,”* then in the nihilist’s eyes *“life is completely amoral”* – anything goes, which historically has provoked fears of despair or even violent outcomes. Little wonder that philosophers have called nihilism *“the greatest crisis of humanity,”* a force that destroys all ethical bearings.

Yet, humans continue to seek ways to live. **Existentialist thinkers** (often in response to nihilism) argued that even if values are not given from above, we are free – and indeed compelled – to **create our own values**. Sartre’s famous dictum *“existence precedes essence”* implies that there is no predefined human nature or moral law; it’s up to us to *invent* meaning and ethics through our choices. Albert Camus, facing a valueless absurd world, proposed an “ethics of rebellion”: by rebelling against injustice and absurdity, we say *“yes”* to certain values (like human dignity) even while knowing there’s no ultimate justification for them. The **rebel**, in Camus’s view, refuses to accept any **unlimited nihilism**; in declaring that there are limits to what we will tolerate, the rebel *“says yes and no simultaneously”*, implicitly affirming a value (justice, or solidarity) in the very act of denying meaninglessness. This is a delicate balance to avoid sliding back into nihilistic destruction or fanaticism. Camus and others thus attempted to build a **human-centered ethics “as if” life had meaning**, emphasizing humility and solidarity to prevent despair and violence.

**Nihiltheism** approaches values by first **fully acknowledging the “value collapse”** that nihilism uncovers. All external or traditional sources of morality (divine commands, absolute laws) are questioned or nullified – a Nihiltheist agrees with Nietzsche that *“there is no objective order… except what we give it”*. However, rather than conclude that *anything* is permissible, Nihiltheism looks for an ethical compass *through* the experience of nothingness. This often means:

- **Personal Authenticity and Responsibility:** If no preset meaning exists, individuals bear responsibility for their actions and the meanings they create. Nihiltheism would encourage an authentic life where one’s values are chosen knowingly in the shadow of the void. This echoes existentialist ethics – *we must become the authors of our values* – but with a spiritual dimension of accountability to the transcendence one seeks.
- **“Value from Void” – Creative Revaluation:** Just as Nietzsche envisioned a “revaluation of all values” after nihilism, Nihiltheism sees the confrontation with nothingness as an opportunity to **create new values**. These would not be arbitrary whims, but values arising from a deep appreciation of life’s fragility and the **interconnectedness revealed by the void**. For example, recognizing the lack of inherent meaning could foster **compassion** (since all of us struggle in the same meaningless predicament) or **freedom** (since no cosmic rules bind us, we can choose to reduce suffering or pursue understanding). Some Eastern philosophies support this: in Buddhism, the realization of emptiness (śūnyatā) leads to compassion for all beings. Nihiltheism similarly might argue that *because* nothing has absolute value, **every choice matters all the more**, as we ourselves are the source of whatever meaning and goodness exists.
- **Ethical Restraint Informed by Nihility:** Interestingly, nihilism can also act as a check on fanaticism. When one accepts that no ideology or moral system has ultimate sanction, it can breed **tolerance and humility**. Nihiltheism would advocate an ethic that is **empathetic and open-minded**, avoiding dogmatic extremes. In Camus’s terms, a Nihiltheist “rebels” against both naive absolutism *and* total moral despair, steering a middle path where one lives by chosen principles (honesty, creativity, non-harm, etc.) while knowing they are human constructs. This *provisional* but sincere morality could be seen as **“meaningful meaninglessness”** – living **as if** life is meaningful, as a conscious strategy to flourish in an indifferent universe.

In summary, the axiological answer Nihiltheism offers is neither a return to absolute laws nor a dive into amoral chaos. It’s a call to **authentic, self-aware value creation**. By confronting the void (where no pre-given values survive) we are challenged to **become “passionately committed”** to values we choose. Importantly, Nihiltheism frames this creation of values within a transcendent quest: ethical living becomes a practice that keeps one aligned with the **Transcendent Nothingness** (for instance, practicing compassion, ascetic simplicity, or honesty might be seen as aligning with the truth of nothingness, since these virtues require ego-reduction and facing reality). Thus, out of *nothing*, a new ethical orientation can emerge – not absolute, but profoundly felt and continually renewed in the light of nihilistic insight.

## Being and Nothingness: The Ontological Question

**Question:***What is the nature of reality or being, if ultimately “nothing” lies at the heart of existence?* This ontological puzzle goes back to the most basic philosophical inquiry: **“Why is there something rather than nothing?”**. Nihilism, especially in its **metaphysical** form, entertains the stark possibility that perhaps nothing *truly* exists in the way we assume. While we obviously live in a world of “somethings,” the nihilistic perspective asserts that there is no inherent **structure or meaning** behind what exists – reality lacks any fundamental value or purpose. Friedrich Nietzsche encapsulated this by saying *“there is no objective order or structure in the world except what we give it”*. In other words, being has no built-in truth; it is we who impose patterns. He even suggested **“there is simply no true world”** hidden behind our illusions. Such statements shake the foundation of ontology: if all being is *phenomenal* (appearance) with no noumenal essence, then **Being itself is “nothing”** in a sense – an emptiness we vainly try to fill with our interpretations.

20th-century existentialists put a twist on this. Sartre’s formula *“existence precedes essence”* means that humans (and perhaps all entities) have no predetermined nature – first we exist, then we define ourselves. This implies that at root, **existence is contingent and void of essence**. Sartre wrote about the **“nothingness” at the heart of Being**, an idea also explored by Heidegger. Heidegger famously stated **“the nothing nothings”**, pointing to nothingness as an active force or context in which beings appear. He observed that modern humanity was in a state of nihilistic “forgetfulness of Being,” where we treat beings as mere objects and lose sight of the **mystery that there is anything at all**. By 1927, Heidegger noted **nihilism had become “the normal state of man,”** dissolving traditional ontologies. In nihilism’s wake, the **ground of being seems to erode** – no God, no Platonic forms, no inherent telos remain to undergird reality. This is what Helmut Thielicke summarized as nihilism’s one truth: *“Ultimately Nothingness prevails and the world is meaningless”*. Ontologically, this suggests that **Nothingness (non-being) is the ultimate reality**, overshadowing all particular beings.

However, is *Nothingness* truly absolute? Western and Eastern philosophies diverge and converge on this point:

- **Western Nihilism’s Abyss:** In Western thought, the nihilistic view of nothingness often appears as an abyss – a void of meaning lurking beneath the surface of things. When Nietzsche declared “God is dead,” he heralded the collapse of the transcendent Being that gave reality coherence. What remains is an “endless nihilation of nihility” – an ongoing process where every attempt to find an absolute ground dissolves. Some, like philosopher Eugene Rose, feared this leads to a *“cold, inhuman world”* of *“incoherence”*, where reality itself feels unreal. Western literature and art have often depicted this dread: the sense that behind our world there is only chaos or nothing (H.P. Lovecraft’s cosmic horror is an extreme imaginative example, positing a universe where humans are insignificant accidents in a vast emptiness or indifferent cosmos).
- **Eastern Emptiness (Śūnyatā):** Interestingly, Asian philosophical traditions have long engaged with “nothingness,” but in a more nuanced or even positive way. Buddhism’s concept of **śūnyatā (emptiness)** teaches that all phenomena are empty of independent self-nature – they exist interdependently and impermanently. This is not seen as a nihilistic negation *of* the world, but rather a truth *about* the world that can liberate us. Zen Buddhism, for example, asserts that when one truly realizes emptiness, one does not fall into despair but experiences reality as **“just thus”** – things are what they are, intimately interconnected, without any fixed essence. As Zen master Nagarjuna put it, *“Emptiness rightly understood is **identical with dependent co-arising**,”* meaning that because everything is empty of self-existence, everything is profoundly interwoven. In Hindu philosophy, the concept of **māyā** (illusion) similarly treats the world of appearances as not ultimately real; the only reality might be Brahman (the absolute) which transcends all concepts – sometimes described via negativa (as “not this, not that”). These traditions handle nothingness differently: rather than a threatening void, emptiness can be **“a fertile nothingness”** where compassion and insight grow.

**Nihiltheism’s Ontology** attempts to bridge these views. It starts with the Western insight that **all our cherished ontologies collapse** under scrutiny – no categorical being is safe from doubt. But it then inquires: could Nothingness itself be the ground *of* a new understanding of Being? Nihiltheism posits that by fully embracing nothingness, we might uncover a **“sacred void”** – a paradoxical realization that the lack of inherent meaning is itself a kind of foundation. This resonates with mystics: Meister Eckhart, the medieval Christian mystic, spoke of God as *nameless nothingness*, the **Godhead beyond God**. He and others used language like **“God is no-thing”** to indicate that the ultimate reality isn’t a being among beings, but an ontological emptiness that is also fullness. Nishitani Keiji, a Japanese philosopher of the Kyoto School, explicitly tackled this in *Religion and Nothingness*. He argued that Western nihilism (which he calls “nihility”) must be passed through and transformed into **true emptiness (sunyata)**. **“Nihilism overcomes itself”** when we stop clinging even to nothingness as a concept and *live* the insight of emptiness. Nishitani found that the idea of emptiness was **“demanded by the problem of nihilism”** – it was the only way to accommodate both the abyss of nihilism and a renewal beyond it. In simpler terms, **Nothingness is not merely an absence, but can become a presence**: a dynamic space in which new meaning or being can emerge.

In practice, Nihiltheism views reality as **non-dual**: the divide between being and nothingness blurs. It might say, echoing Zen, that *form is emptiness and emptiness is form*. The everyday world is “empty” of ultimate meaning, yet that very emptiness is what allows for **any meaning at all to be created**. Some Nihiltheist principles describe nothingness as a **creative principle** – for instance, the revolutionary nihilist Bakunin once wrote *“the passion for destruction is also a creative passion!”*, implying that tearing down illusions clears the ground for new life. Nihiltheism takes this creatively: by **“destroying” our attachment to fixed beliefs (annihilating idols), we open up a space for a freer, more fluid reality**. In that sense, Nothingness or the Void is almost like **the canvas on which existence paints itself** – empty, but pregnant with possibility.

Thus, ontologically, Nihiltheism stands between pure nihilism and spiritual ontology. It agrees that there is no *substantial* essence or predetermined order (so, in a way, *Nothingness reigns*). But it also suggests that Nothingness *nourishes* a transcendent perspective: when we recognize the illusory nature of what we take as reality, we can engage with existence in a new, more authentic way. The world seen as illusory is not dismissed; rather, it becomes a **manifestation of the void** – a place where, precisely because nothing is fixed, everything is open to transformation. This is a key ontological insight of Nihiltheism: **Being is transient and groundless, but that groundlessness is itself the ground of a higher understanding**. In the “universe of Nihiltheism,” to exist is to be dancing on the edge of the void – and perhaps, finding that the void *also* is the ground of being, one and the same as a transcendent reality in disguise.

## Despair and Meaninglessness: The Phenomenological Question

**Question:***How do humans experience a world without inherent meaning? What is it like to confront the “void,” and how should we deal with the feelings that result?* This is the phenomenological and existential concern – it’s about the **lived experience** of nihilism. When one truly realizes (or feels) that life has no predetermined meaning or value, a range of profound emotions and existential moods can flood in. Classic existentialism and literature have vividly described these: **despair, anxiety, absurdity, alienation, nihilistic dread**, even **“cosmic horror.”** Nihilism is not just an abstract theory; it often comes as a *personal crisis*. As the **IEP** notes, *“when we abandon illusions, life is revealed as nothing”*, and this nothingness is the source of *“existential horror and emotional anguish.”* Each person finds themselves *“an isolated being ‘thrown’ into an alien and unresponsive universe, barred forever from knowing why”* – a condition deemed **absurd**. This feeling of absurdity, famously articulated by Albert Camus, is like **the plight of Sisyphus**, eternally rolling a boulder uphill only to see it roll down again. It captures the sense of futile struggle in a world that “does not answer.”

Key phenomenological states associated with nihilism include:

- **Despair and Depression:** As early as the 19th century, Kierkegaard wrote about the “sickness unto death” (despair) that comes from feeling one’s life has no grounding. In nihilism, despair arises when all goals and values seem void – **“the highest values devalue themselves”** and *“‘Why’ finds no answer”*. This can manifest as a deep **weariness or depression**: Shakespeare’s character Macbeth soliloquized that life is nothing more than *“a tale told by an idiot… signifying nothing”* – a bleak, disenchanted view of existence. Prolonged exposure to this outlook can lead to hopelessness or apathy.
- **Anxiety and Angst:** Existentialists distinguished *despair* (about meaninglessness) from *anxiety* or *angst*, which is more a raw dread or dizziness in face of absolute freedom and nothingness. Without any given purpose, we face **the “abyss of freedom”** – we alone are responsible for whatever we become, which can be terrifying. Heidegger described an uncanny anxiety that arises when we momentarily see through the routines of life and sense “the nothing” beneath. This angst has no object – it’s not fear of something, but a general condition of *groundlessness*. It signals that nothingness is “at the heart” of our being.
- **Absurdity and Irony:** Camus’ term *“the Absurd”* summarizes the confrontation between our mind’s search for meaning and the indifferent silence of the universe. The absurd is often accompanied by dark **irony or humor** – a sense that life’s attempts at significance are almost comically futile. Some individuals respond with bitter laughter, defiance, or by embracing the absurd heroism (Camus imagines Sisyphus happy, defiantly rolling his stone in full awareness of its absurdity). Yet, this is a fragile triumph; as Camus and others admitted, sustaining joy or commitment in an absurd world is an ongoing struggle.
- **Nihilistic Angst in Culture (Cosmic Horror):** In the 20th century, beyond philosophy, the genre of cosmic horror (e.g. H.P. Lovecraft) gave voice to these feelings. Here the protagonist often discovers some cosmic truth – namely, that humanity is insignificant in an uncaring vast cosmos – and this drives them to madness or existential terror. Such stories are extreme metaphors for the nihilistic realization of our minuscule, *accidental* place in the universe. The **“existential despair fueled by cosmic insignificance”** is a hallmark of this horror genre. While fictional, it reflects a real modern mood: the more we learn from science about the enormous, impersonal universe, the easier it is to feel adrift in **a cold void**.

The **common thread**, as observed in existentialist literature, is that all these reactions – despair, anxiety, absurdity – are responses to **the confrontation with nothingness**. The question then becomes: *can we survive this confrontation?* Can we find a way to live with these feelings without collapsing into misery or nihilistic self-destruction? The existentialists struggled mightily here. They hoped the answer was **“Yes”** – that one could stare into the abyss and still live authentically – but their answers were often tentative. They suggested things like *“passionate commitment”* (pouring oneself into creative projects, love, etc.) combined with *stoic acceptance* of what cannot be changed. In effect, they proposed we give ourselves our own meaning. However, as the IEP puts it, these solutions were *“tinged with desperation”*, because **in an absurd world with no guidelines, “any course of action is problematic”**. They knew that even our chosen meanings are essentially groundless. This led some to conclude that living authentically with full knowledge of life’s meaninglessness might be *nearly impossible*.

**Nihiltheism acknowledges this human plight in full:** it does not sugarcoat the depth of **existential despair** that true nihilistic insight can provoke. In fact, one of its principles is that instead of fleeing these dark emotions, we should **“deeply feel and experience” the despair** that accompanies recognizing life’s emptiness. The idea is that by going *through* despair rather than around it, we can potentially break through to a new perspective. This is analogous to spiritual “dark nights of the soul” in mysticism, where losing all familiar comforts and meanings is seen as a prerequisite to a profound transformation. Nihiltheism encourages practices of **confrontation and endurance**: for example, contemplative solitude, meditation on mortality, or even psychedelic voyages (entheogens) that dissolve the ego. These can induce a kind of **“ego dissolution”** where one directly faces the nullity of the self and the world. Such experiences are daunting, but they can also yield insight – many mystical traditions speak of a breakthrough where intense despair flips into illumination or peace.

In more everyday terms, Nihiltheism would advise cultivating an **acceptance of absurdity** and learning to find a form of contentment within it. This could mean adopting a stance of **tragic optimism** – acknowledging the worst (that there’s no inherent meaning, that we will die and be forgotten, etc.) yet still affirming life. It aligns with Camus’ call to imagine Sisyphus happy. Likewise, it resonates with certain Buddhist practices: mindfulness of suffering and impermanence, which initially can cause sorrow, but ultimately lead to compassion and equanimity. If everything is truly transient and without fixed meaning, then *every moment* becomes precious in its uniqueness. **Nihiltheistic practice** might involve reminding oneself of the void not to despair more, but to strip away false comforts and thereby live **more genuinely**.

Practically, one might develop personal rituals or creative expressions to cope. Art and literature can be powerful outlets – many nihilistic or absurdist artists found that by *creating* (even in a void), they assert their freedom and connect with others. Community, too, helps: sharing our existential anxieties with fellow seekers can alleviate the isolation (the feeling of being “thrown alone” into the cosmos). Nihiltheism as a budding movement emphasizes forming an **“existential community”** where people jointly explore these dark questions, support each other through bouts of nihilistic dread, and perhaps laugh together at the absurdity (a kind of gallows humor solidarity).

In sum, the phenomenological answer of Nihiltheism to despair is: **do not flee it, transform it**. By accepting despair, anxiety, and meaninglessness as authentic parts of the human condition, we rob them of some of their terror. We might even find a strange **exhilaration in the freedom** that comes with having nothing to lose. Nihiltheism holds that after one fully says “yes” to the void – *“gazes into the abyss”* and does not blink – one may find that the abyss gazes back with a sort of understanding. In that understanding, new forms of meaning (self-made, transient, compassionate) can be cultivated. The **pain is real** – *“the corrosion of nihilism is vexing and corrosive”* – but it is also the crucible for spiritual growth in this framework. As a result, the lived experience of nihilism, while harsh, becomes the very path by which a Nihiltheist seeks transcendence (as we explore next).

## Transcendence and the Sacred: The Spiritual Question

**Question:***Is there anything beyond the “nothingness” of nihilism? Can we speak of God, the divine, or a transcendent reality in a nihilistic framework?* This final question is crucial: it asks whether nihilism’s “nothing” is the last word, or if that very nothingness could be the gateway to a new kind of spiritual understanding. **Nihiltheism** is built around this possibility. The term itself combines *nihilism* (“nothing”) with *theism* (belief in God or the divine). On the surface, those seem contradictory – how can one be both nihilistic and theistic? The answer lies in **redefining transcendence** in light of nothingness.

Traditionally, **Theism** asserts a transcendent reality: God or gods who imbue the universe with order, meaning, and morality. Nihilism, by contrast, arises strongly in the absence or rejection of such a belief – Nietzsche’s announcement of God’s death symbolized the loss of all transcendent guarantors of meaning. A straightforward nihilist says: *No God, no higher purpose, we’re alone in an indifferent void.* The nihilist either stops there or perhaps finds personal freedom in this revelation. **Nihiltheism, however, does not stop at the void.** It hypothesizes that the void *itself* might be experienced as **transcendent** or divine in a non-traditional sense.

This idea is foreshadowed in various mystic and philosophical sources:

- **Mystical Nothingness:** Many mystics in different religions have described encountering God as a kind of nothingness or emptiness. The **apophatic (negative) theology** of Pseudo-Dionysius, Meister Eckhart, and others insists that we can only say what God is *not*, for God transcends all categories. Eckhart spoke of the **“Godhead”** beyond the personal God, a state of absolute emptiness or desert. He even said *“I pray God to rid me of God,”* meaning the highest experience of the divine is one where all our images of God vanish into a silent darkness. In these accounts, encountering the divine can feel like entering **a great void** – but paradoxically, it is a void full of peace, bliss, or supreme reality. As one scholar put it, for Eckhart *“absolute nothingness signals the point at which all modes of being are transcended”*. In Jewish mysticism too, the concept of **“Ayin” (nothingness)** is the highest sephira (Kabbalistic emanation of God), indicating God’s hidden aspect as No-thing. Thus, there is a long tradition of seeing **divinity in the nothing**.
- **Eastern Enlightenment:** Eastern spiritual traditions, especially Buddhism and Taoism, have an analogous theme. Enlightenment in Mahayana Buddhism is often described as the realization of **śūnyatā** (emptiness). Far from being a nihilistic termination, this realization is said to bring about a profound liberation and compassion – it’s considered seeing things as they truly are, without ego or illusion. Zen Buddhism deliberately uses negation and nothingness (think of koans like “What is the sound of one hand clapping?” or the concept of **mu (no, or nothing)**) to break the mind free of conceptual cages. The experience of **satori** (awakening) in Zen is sometimes characterized as a direct encounter with the void – and discovering the void is not frightening but the very ground of one’s Buddha-nature. Likewise, Taoism speaks of the Tao as an **empty vessel**, the **“Nameless”** that is the origin of heaven and earth. Laozi said, *“Empty yourself of everything – let the mind become still.”* In that emptiness, one aligns with the Tao. All these suggest that **transcendence might emerge from profound nothingness** rather than from a being or entity.

Nihiltheism takes inspiration from such insights. It proposes that **by fully immersing in nihilism (the recognition of nothingness), one can paradoxically encounter a Transcendent Reality**. As one summary puts it, *“Nihilism…acknowledges \[meaninglessness\] but seeks to actively engage with it, rather than avoid it”*. This engagement can lead to moments of breakthrough. Nihiltheism suggests that while we, as finite humans, **“cannot fully transcend Nihilism”**, we *can* have **“fleeting glimpses of the Transcendent”**. In the Nihiltheist view, these *could* be genuine if brief contacts with the Transcendent Nothingness that underlies existence. In such non-dual awareness, nothingness and fullness become one; some describe it as **plenum void** (empty yet full). The **illusion of a separate self** is seen through, which can bring profound peace and insight.

Nihiltheism thus does not offer a personal God watching over us – that notion is largely incompatible with its nihilistic starting point (which rejects naive theism). They might describe that state as encountering the **Transcendent** or even the **Divine**, but notably it was accessed through *nothingness*, not by clinging to a belief. In afterward reflection, they may conclude that what we call “God” could simply be this state of **absolute nothingness full of potential**. In Nishitani’s terms, they moved from the field of nihility to the field of sunyata.
