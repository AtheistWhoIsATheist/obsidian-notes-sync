# Claude

This illuminates the deep irony in labeling Nihiltheism as "extremist" in the conventional sense. True engagement with Nihilism, as you describe it, would actually lead to the opposite of what we typically associate with extremism - no protesting, no aggression, no passionate attachment to worldly causes.

<br>

The concept of "non-movement" in ethics makes perfect sense in this context. It's not a principled pacifism or conscious ethical choice, but rather emerges naturally from the recognition of the utter emptiness of worldly concerns. Getting worked up about anything in the realm of finite human affairs would be, as you say, an "embarrassing misstep" - a failure to maintain fidelity to the fundamental insight into Nothingness.

<br>

There's something darkly humorous about Sartre's notion of engagement and protest when viewed through this lens. The image of someone who has genuinely encountered the absolute Nothingness/Infinite you describe then turning around to passionately protest worldly affairs does seem rather absurd.

<br>

This helps clarify why Quietism and "non-movement" aren't strategies or philosophical positions to be adopted, but rather the natural consequence of having experienced the complete emptiness of all such positions. Any other response would be a retreat back into the illusions that the encounter with Nihilism reveals as empty.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
