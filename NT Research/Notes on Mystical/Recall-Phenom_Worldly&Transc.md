## Temporal Dissolution and Mystical Insights

## The Synthesis of Despair, Faith, and Doubt in Nihiltheistic Thought

- The concept of the Transcendent is described as an encounter with the "Wholly Other", a reality that is fundamentally different from ordinary experience and eludes precise definition, often leading to silence or paradoxical expressions in attempts to describe it.

- The concept of "Nothingness" is used to capture the essence of the Transcendent as an ontological void, a fertile void from which all existence arises and to which it returns, and is also used to describe the experiential abyss encountered in mystical union, where distinctions between self and other, subject and object, vanish.
- The term "Infinite Presence" is also used to convey aspects of the Transcendent, capturing the overwhelming sense of reality and immediacy that characterizes it, but "Nothingness" remains the most precise descriptor within the framework of Nihiltheism, reflecting both the paradoxical and apophatic nature of the Transcendent.
- The use of "Nothingness" to describe the Transcendent is justified through various philosophical and mystical approaches, including Heidegger's exploration of the nothing as the ground of Being, Cioran's mystical Nihilism, and the tradition of Apophatic theology, which emphasizes describing the divine by negation.
- The reports of mystics across traditions, including Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, often describe their encounters with the Transcendent in terms that negate ordinary reality, pointing to "Nothingness" as the most appropriate term for the ineffable reality they experience.
