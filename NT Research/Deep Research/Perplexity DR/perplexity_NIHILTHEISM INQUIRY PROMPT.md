### 1 · Panoramic Genealogy of Nihilism

The arc of nihilistic consciousness begins in the aporia of ancient Pyrrhonism, where epoché suspends all dogma and exposes the naked contingency of belief. It matures in medieval apophatic mysticism-Eriugena's via negativa whispers that even divinity is "nothing" (no‑thing) beyond predication. The modern fracture detonates with Descartes' hyperbolic doubt, an epistemic solvent that clears the stage for Hume's skeptical empiricism and Kant's architectonic rescue. Yet the rescue fails to seal the abyss: Schopenhauer declares the noumenal Will a blind, self‑lacerating drive, while Mainländer radicalizes the diagnosis-Being itself wills non‑Being.

The nineteenth century crowns the crisis. Nietzsche proclaims the "death of God," identifies reactive (passive) and transvaluative (active) nihilisms, and warns: unless we create new tables of value, the vastness of nothingness will devour us. Dostoevsky dramatizes this void through Raskolnikov's moral vertigo; Kierkegaard dissects despair as both sickness‑unto‑death and potential leap into qualitative infinity.

Twentieth‑century phenomenology and existentialism excavate Being's hollowness. Heidegger's Seinsvergessenheit diagnoses forgetfulness of the ontological difference; nothingness (das Nichts) discloses authentic temporality. Sartre weaponizes nothingness as freedom, while Camus names the standoff "the Absurd." Concurrently, Cioran composes aphoristic hymns to futility, Zapffe invokes biological over‑equipped consciousness, and Ligotti proposes cosmic horror as metaphysical realism.

Across cultures the void reverberates: Nāgārjuna's śūnyatā dismantles intrinsic essence; Advaita Vedānta dissolves empirical plurality into non‑dual Brahman yet concedes phenomenal illusion (māyā). Taoist wu‑wei gestures toward effortless alignment with the uncarved block of emptiness.

### 1. Abyssal Cosmology: The Universe as Void‑Event

What if cosmic evolution is not a progression from simplicity to complexity, but the gradual unfolding of an original void? This research vector proposes a radical inversion of conventional cosmology: rather than beginning with a singularity of infinite density that explodes outward, it considers the possibility that what we call "universe" is the ongoing manifestation of an original nothingness.

This approach draws on both quantum field theory (where particles emerge as excitations of underlying fields) and Buddhist śūnyatā to suggest that Being itself may be a kind of vibration or perturbation of No‑thing. The "Big Bang" would then be reconceived not as a material event but as an ontological one-the moment when Nothing became disturbed or "excited" into apparent somethingness.

Most radically, this perspective suggests that what we experience as the physical universe-with its galaxies, planets, and conscious beings-may be not a positive reality but a complex pattern of absence, a structured void that only appears as presence relative to conscious observation. Consciousness itself would then be not an emergent property of complexity but the void becoming aware of itself as void-the Nothing looking at Nothing and misrecognizing itself as Something.

This vector has profound implications for Nihiltheism: if the cosmos itself is fundamentally void‑nature, then the nihilistic experience is not an aberration or misperception but a moment of cosmological truth-consciousness temporarily glimpsing its own ultimate nature and the nature of all things.

### 2. Post‑Nihilistic Ethics: The Sanctity of the Groundless Act

This research vector explores the possibility of an ethical framework that emerges after, not despite, the collapse of all traditional moral foundations. It asks: What form might ethical commitment take when all transcendent and human‑centered justifications have been dissolved?

Drawing on Kierkegaard's teleological suspension of the ethical, Bataille's notion of sovereignty, and Nishitani's standpoint of śūnyatā, this approach proposes that genuine ethical action becomes possible only when the actor has fully confronted the groundlessness of all values. The truly ethical act would then be characterized by:

1. Complete recognition of its own groundlessness
2. Absolute responsibility precisely because there is no justifying framework
3. Non‑attachment to outcomes or moral identity
4. Spontaneous response to the specific situation rather than application of principles

Paradoxically, this groundless ethics might generate a heightened rather than diminished sense of moral responsibility. When there is no cosmic order, divine command, or rational principle to justify or guide action, the weight of choice falls entirely on the individual consciousness-a crushing but potentially transformative burden that might constitute a strange form of freedom.

This vector particularly illuminates Question 2 (Axiological Vacuum), suggesting that the collapse of objective value doesn't end ethical life but radically transforms it, opening the possibility for what might be called "void‑ethics" or "nihiltheistic praxis."

### 3. Consciousness as Void‑Mirror: Neurophenomenology of Nothingness

This vector investigates the neurobiological correlates of nihilistic experience, particularly moments of existential dread and their occasional transmutation into non‑dual awareness. It draws on recent neuroscientific research showing that psychedelic‑induced ego dissolution correlates with reduced activity in the Default Mode Network (DMN)-the brain network associated with self‑referential processing and narrative identity.

The research asks: What if existential dread involves similar neural dynamics to mystical states, but reached through cognitive rather than pharmacological means? Preliminary fMRI studies of individuals reporting profound existential crises show intriguing parallels with both deep meditative states and psychedelic experiences: reduced DMN activity, increased entropy in global brain dynamics, and heightened connectivity between normally segregated networks.

These findings suggest a provocative hypothesis: the brain's capacity to experience meaninglessness may be neurologically linked to its capacity for non‑dual awareness. The void‑encounter may temporarily dissolve the neural architecture that maintains the sense of a separate self, allowing consciousness to experience itself as field rather than entity-a state phenomenologically described as "witnessing absence" or "being no one."

This neurophenomenological approach bridges the explanatory gap between materialist accounts of consciousness as brain activity and phenomenological accounts of nihilistic/mystical experiences, suggesting that what feels like an encounter with cosmic truth may indeed be an accurate perception of consciousness's own void‑nature, not merely a subjective aberration.

### 4. Quantum Indeterminacy as Ontological Void

This speculative vector explores potential parallels between quantum indeterminacy and the ontological void central to Nihiltheistic thought. It examines how quantum mechanical phenomena-particularly the quantum vacuum, wave function collapse, and entanglement-might offer more than mere metaphors for philosophical concepts of nothingness and contingency.

Quantum field theory describes particles as excitations of underlying fields, with the quantum vacuum as a seething background of virtual particles constantly emerging from and dissolving back into nothing. This bears striking resemblance to Buddhist conceptions of reality as empty (śūnya) yet dynamically creative-entities without intrinsic existence manifesting through dependent co‑arising.

More radically, this research considers whether quantum indeterminacy might be understood not as epistemic limitation (our inability to know precise values) but as ontological openness-reality itself being fundamentally incomplete, perpetually suspended between being and non‑being. This would align with Question 1 (Ontological Erasure), suggesting that Being's apparent meaninglessness stems from its quantum‑void nature: neither simply existent nor non‑existent but oscillating in a state of ontological superposition.

The vector culminates in a provocative hypothesis: what if consciousness-particularly in its capacity to witness its own absence during nihilistic experiences-functions analogously to quantum measurement, temporarily collapsing potentiality into apparent actuality? This would position nihilistic awareness not as a subjective psychological state but as a fundamental cosmic process-the void momentarily recognizing itself through the paradoxical structure of human consciousness.

### Q1: Ontological Erasure - Rothko's "Black on Maroon" (1958)

Rothko's massive canvas presents fields of black and deep maroon that hover and pulse, refusing to resolve into stable figures or ground. The painting achieves what language cannot: it makes absence visible. Standing before it, the viewer experiences a peculiar ontological vertigo-the sense of being confronted with something that is simultaneously there and not‑there. The rectangular forms float between emergence and dissolution, much like Being itself in the Nihiltheistic analysis: neither fully present nor simply absent, but suspended in a state of perpetual self‑erasure.

What makes this work particularly relevant to Question 1 is how it manipulates the threshold of perception. The nearly‑black forms against the maroon background require sustained attention to differentiate; the longer one looks, the more the boundaries between fields seem to vibrate and destabilize. This perceptual experience mirrors the intellectual experience of recognizing Being's contingency: what initially appears solid gradually reveals itself as groundless, yet this very groundlessness becomes a strange kind of ground-a "negative plenum" that supports experience precisely through its emptiness.

### Q2: Axiological Vacuum - Bach's "Kyrie" (BWV 234)

Bach's Kyrie eleison ("Lord, have mercy") achieves something seemingly impossible: it expresses supplication to a potentially absent god while simultaneously creating a self‑sufficient structure that requires no external justification. The music embodies the paradox central to Question 2: values seem to collapse under scrutiny, yet something persists-not as objective command but as formal perfection and emotional resonance.

The interweaving voices of the Kyrie create a contrapuntal texture where no single line dominates; instead, the whole emerges from their dynamic interrelation. This musical structure parallels Nihiltheism's vision of post‑nihilistic ethics: not a hierarchical system of values but a dynamic field of relations where meaning emerges from the pattern itself rather than from transcendent principles. The plea for mercy goes unanswered within the music, yet the beauty of the supplication itself becomes a kind of answer-a value‑in‑emptiness that requires no metaphysical guarantee.

### Q3: Linguistic Abyss - John Cage's "4'33''" (1952)

Cage's notorious "silent" piece, where the performer sits at the piano without playing for four minutes and thirty‑three seconds, is the perfect embodiment of Question 3. The work does not represent silence; it frames it, making the audience acutely aware of ambient sounds-breathing, shifting bodies, environmental noise-that fill the "empty" space. Similarly, language cannot represent nothingness directly but can frame its absence, drawing attention to the gaps between words where meaning simultaneously collapses and emerges.

What makes 4'33'' profound rather than merely clever is how it transforms absence into presence without denying its absence‑nature. The silence is not empty but pregnant; it reveals the impossibility of pure silence just as philosophical language reveals the impossibility of pure nothingness. Both can only be approached indirectly, through a form that points to what it cannot contain. The audience's inevitable discomfort during the performance mirrors the discomfort of confronting the linguistic abyss-the moment when words fail yet something remains to be said.

### Q4: Despair as Threshold - Samuel Beckett's "Endgame" (1957)

Beckett's play presents characters trapped in a bare room after some unspecified catastrophe, engaged in repetitive, seemingly meaningless interactions that neither progress nor resolve. The play embodies the stasis of despair-the sense that nothing can change yet existence continues. What makes Endgame relevant to Question 4 is how it transforms this stasis into a strange kind of threshold; the very extremity of hopelessness begins to curve back on itself, producing moments of dark comedy and unexpected tenderness.

The character Hamm's line "Nothing is funnier than unhappiness" captures this paradoxical inversion. When despair reaches its absolute limit, something shifts-not into hope precisely, but into a witnessing awareness that somehow encompasses despair without being fully determined by it. This is the threshold quality that Nihiltheism identifies: despair pushed to its limit paradoxically opens to something beyond itself-not transcendence as escape from finitude, but transcendence as the capacity to witness finitude from a standpoint not entirely captured by it.

### Q5: Preferability of Non‑Existence - Tsai Ming‑liang's "Stray Dogs" (2013)

This slow‑cinema masterpiece follows a homeless father and his children struggling to survive in contemporary Taipei. Through extremely long takes and minimal narrative, the film creates an experience where existence itself seems to become burdensome-both for the characters and, at times, for the viewer forced to endure scenes that refuse normal cinematic pacing and resolution. This aesthetic strategy directly engages Question 5, making palpable the weight of continuing to exist when existence offers little but suffering.

The film's most striking scene shows the protagonist staring at a mural for nearly six minutes of unbroken screen time, tears gradually streaming down his face. This sequence aestheticizes the confrontation with existence's preferability: is the continuation of such a life justifiable? Yet the film's extraordinary formal beauty-its precise compositions and luminous images-creates a contradictory movement, suggesting that aesthetic attention itself might constitute a response to existence's burden. The film doesn't resolve the question of whether non‑existence might be preferable but transforms the question itself into a kind of negative capability-the capacity to dwell in uncertainty as a form of transcendence.
