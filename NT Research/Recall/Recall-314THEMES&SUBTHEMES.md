## 1. Cultural and Historical Explanations Are Not Sufficient  

### The universality, depth, and recurrence of nihilistic experience cannot be explained away by context—these thinkers make clear their condition transcends cultural particulars.  

---  

### St. Augustine  

- “At this grief my heart was utterly darkened; and whatever I beheld was death.”  
- “...wretched is every soul bound by the friendship of perishable things.”   
*(A direct confrontation with meaninglessness, not merely a product of his time but a perennial existential crisis.)*  

---  

### Teresa of Avila  

- “The best of created things disappoint and torment the soul...God alone satisfies the soul.”  
- “All that such souls ever attain to and perform, all these things only make them more humble, and therefore more strong; more able to despise the things of this world...”  
- “World of light. I tell you it was a piteous and a dreadful thing to see. I knew not where to hide myself, for that shining light, in which was no darkness at all, held the whole world within it, and all worlds. You will see that I could not flee from its presence.”   
*(Her vision of the “whole world within it, and all worlds” is not culturally confined, but existentially universal.)*  

---  

### Edgar Saltus (on Leopardi)  

- “The suffering which he experienced was not, he was very sure, limited solely to himself. It was, he considered, the garment and appanage of every sentient being.”  
- “In brief, then, life to the Christian is a probation, to the Brahmin a burden, to the Buddhist a dream, and to the pessimist a nightmare.”   
*(The universality of suffering is not reducible to a single tradition—it pervades all outlooks.)*  

---  

### Miguel de Unamuno  

- “Contradiction! Of course! Since we only live in and by contradictions, since life is tragedy and the tragedy is perpetual struggle, without victory or the hope of victory, life is contradiction.”  
- “And I am convinced that we should solve many things if we all went out into the streets and uncovered our griefs, which perhaps would prove to be but one sole common grief, and joined together in beweeping them and crying aloud to the heavens and calling upon God.”   
*(Human tragedy as an ontological constant, not a historical accident.)*

## 2. The Positive Ontological Status of Nihilism  

### Nihilism is not simply an absence or negation, but a positive, existential condition—sometimes even a privileged access to the real or the divine.  

---  

### Thomas Merton  

- “The truly sacred attitude toward life is in no sense an escape from the sense of nothingness that assails us when we are left alone with ourselves. On the contrary, it penetrates into that darkness and that nothingness, realizing that the mercy of God has transformed our nothingness into His temple and believing that in our darkness His light has hidden itself.”   
*(Nothingness is transfigured into sacred presence—a positive status, not merely a void.)*  

---  

### Martin Luther  

- “Thus, when God makes alive, He does it by killing; when He justifies, He does it by bringing in guilty: when He exalts to Heaven, He does it by bringing down to hell: as the Scripture saith, ‘The Lord killeth and maketh alive, He bringeth down to the grave and raiseth up, (I Sam. ii. 6.)’ … Thus He conceals His eternal mercy and loving-kindness behind His eternal wrath: His righteousness, behind apparent iniquity.”   
*(Negation, darkness, and even despair are the very means through which ultimate reality is disclosed.)*  

---  

### Meister Eckhart  

- (Not directly quoted above, but in this tradition, “the ground of the soul” is often described as a nothingness or desert where union with God becomes possible. The Journal314 corpus repeatedly returns to this theme.)

## 3. Nihilism as Fundamental Human Experience (Yet Rare)  

### Nihilism is depicted not as pathology, but as the deepest, rarest encounter with existence—often reserved for the few willing to see without illusion.  

---  

### Edgar Saltus (on Leopardi)  

- “His logic reduced itself to the paraphrase of an axiom, ‘I am, therefore I suffer,’ and the suffering which he experienced was not, he was very sure, limited solely to himself. It was, he considered, the garment and appanage of every sentient being.”  
- “In the world ye shall have tribulation,” is the explicit warning of the Founder of Christianity, and to this warning all creeds, save that of the early Hellenists, concur. It did not, therefore, come from any religious teaching, nor, for that matter, from any philosophy.”   
*(Suffering and nihilistic encounter are intrinsic to sentience, but few recognize it fully.)*  

---  

### Kempis (Imitation of Christ)  

- “There is naturally in every man a desire to know, but what profiteth knowledge without the fear of God? Better of a surety is a lowly peasant who serveth God, than a proud philosopher who watcheth the stars and neglecteth the knowledge of himself.”  
- “So long as we live in the world, we cannot be without trouble and trial.”   
*(Universal, but truly grasped only in rare contemplative or broken moments.)*  

---  

### Miguel de Unamuno  

- “We all lack something; only some of us feel the lack and others do not. Or they pretend not to feel the lack, and then they are hypocrites.”   
*(The rare are those who see the abyss, most hide from it.)*

## 4. Nihilism as the Temporal Expression of the Transcendent / God  

### Many of these figures explicitly or implicitly describe the nihilistic encounter as the only true pathway to the Transcendent or as its very temporal appearance.  

---  

### Theresa of Avila  

- “In prayer there would sometimes come upon me such a sense of the Presence of God that I seemed to be all engulfed in God. I think the learned call this mystical experience; at any rate, it so suspends the ordinary operations of the soul that she seems to be wholly taken out of herself.”  
- “Now this is the difference between natural and supernatural prayer: … In pure supernatural and transcendental prayer, we do nothing at all. His Divine Majesty it is who does it all. He works in us at such elect seasons what far transcends and overtops all the powers and resources even of the renewed nature.”   
*(Divine presence appears through suspension, annihilation, or the abyssal experience.)*  

---  

### Thomas Merton  

- “...it penetrates into that darkness and that nothingness, realizing that the mercy of God has transformed our nothingness into His temple and believing that in our darkness His light has hidden itself.”   
*(God and nothingness, darkness and divine mercy—transcendence appears as and through nihilism.)*  

---  

### Martin Luther  

- “Thus, when God makes alive, He does it by killing; when He justifies, He does it by bringing in guilty: when He exalts to Heaven, He does it by bringing down to hell...”   
*(The experience of nihilism is the medium, not the barrier, of transcendence.)*

## CONTRARIAN ATTACK ON “NIHILISM AS ONTOLOGICAL CONSTANT”  

---  

### 1. “Universal” or Merely Over-Interpreted?  

**Objection:**   
What is called “nihilism” in each figure is often radically different—filtered through unique metaphysical, theological, and psychological frameworks.  

- *Augustine’s* “darkness” is a drama of personal sin and salvation, not ontological emptiness.  
- *Buddhism’s* “emptiness” (śūnyatā) is not nihilistic despair, but a technical doctrine about the lack of inherent essence—distinct from Western existential dread.  
- *Nietzsche’s* abyss is a product of post-Christian loss, not a timeless constant.  

**Counter:**   
Lumping all these under one “ontological constant” risks flattening genuine diversity and nuance in favor of an artificial metaphysical unity.  

---  

### 2. The Problem of Historical Context  

**Objection:**   
Every quote is the child of its time, culture, language.  

- *Ecclesiastes* was shaped by post-exilic disillusionment;  
- *Schopenhauer’s* pessimism arises from 19th-century German Romanticism;  
- *Ligotti* is saturated with late-modern horror fiction and philosophical pessimism.  

To claim universality is to ignore the formative power of epoch and environment. These are *not* all describing the same thing—just analogous moods in different guises.  

---  

### 3. Is Suffering Really Universal?  

**Objection:**   
For many, life is not characterized by suffering or existential dread but by routine, pleasure, and community.  

- Countless traditions emphasize joy, gratitude, and celebration (see Sufi poetry, Confucian virtue ethics, Christian resurrection hymns).  
- The “default mode” for most of humanity is ordinary struggle, not philosophical nihilism.  

To universalize suffering is to mistake the extreme for the norm and the testimony of a minority for that of the species.  

---  

### 4. The Dangers of Selective Quotation  

**Objection:**   
You’ve cherry-picked the most negative or void-centric lines, ignoring the surrounding affirmations of life, faith, or even mundane optimism that fill out these texts.  

- *Kierkegaard* also writes of hope, faith, and love;  
- *Tolstoy* finds meaning in simple living;  
- *Buddha* prescribes a path out of suffering, not into it.  

Context-free extraction distorts the intent of the thinkers and traditions.  

---  

### 5. Philosophical Sleight-of-Hand:  

**Objection:**   
There’s an equivocation between:  

- Ontological nihilism (the idea that Being itself is void/lack),  
- Psychological nihilism (subjective despair or meaninglessness),  
- Moral/ethical nihilism (the denial of value).  

You have collapsed these into one, ignoring that many traditions would violently reject such conflations.  

---  

### 6. What of Affirmation, Creation, and Joy?  

**Objection:**  

- *Nietzsche* himself moved beyond nihilism to affirmation (“Yes-saying”).  
- *Renaissance humanism*, *Sikhism*, *Taoist* nonchalance, and *Zen* humor all point to forms of being that *transcend* or *sublate* the void without being consumed by it.  

Why privilege the void over these other equally universal human responses?  

---  

### 7. Agency, Social Embeddedness, and Evolution  

**Objection:**   
To posit suffering and nihilism as *constants* ignores both the evolutionary role of optimism (Becker’s “denial of death”) and the resilience of communities to cultivate meaning, joy, and resistance to despair.  

Humans have always been myth-makers and world-builders, not just abyss-gazers.  

---  

### 8. Reductive Universalism:  

**Objection:**   
Your meta-synthesis courts the error of **reductive universalism**:  

- It erases pluralism, local color, contradiction, and the full spectrum of philosophical/religious response to the void.  
- Genuine universality would require as much attention to affirmation, transformation, and meaning-making as to suffering and loss.  

---  

### 9. Possible Conclusion of Contrarian Mode:  

If “Nihilism as Ontological Constant” is to stand, it must answer these critiques:  

- Demonstrate, not merely assert, *in what sense* these experiences are the “same.”  
- Show that suffering is not just *possible* but *inescapable* —for *all*, not just the rare philosopher or mystic.  
- Explain why *affirmative*, creative, or transcendent responses do not falsify the claim of nihilism’s primacy.
