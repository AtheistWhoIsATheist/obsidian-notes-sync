- Key characteristics of this stage include a loss of grip on reality, where the external world may seem unreal, dreamlike, or distorted, as well as cognitive and perceptual distortions, such as thought loops, racing thoughts, intense visuals, and synesthesia, which are reminiscent of descriptions by MasterClass, Leary, and Typology.
- The ego's defenses weaken, leading to a fragmentation of self-constructs, where core beliefs about oneself, personal history, and identity start to unravel, and the narrative self loses its coherence, as discussed in PMC - Self Unbound and Frontiers - DIED phenomenology.
- This stage is also marked by bodily disorientation, intensified emotional lability, ranging from profound fear and paranoia to moments of awe or detachment, and a feeling of dying or annihilation, which is a common and often terrifying experience, as noted by Grof and Frontiers - DIED phenomenology.
- The characteristics of this stage strongly align with Category 5: The Dissolving Void of the Typology of Abyssal Encounters, and can also overlap with Category 1: The Traumatic Void and Category 2: The Desolating Void, particularly in terms of diminished self, loss of boundaries, and emotional intensity.

## Live Metaphors and Paradox
- The process of "stripping away" in Category 4: The Kenotic Void also begins in earnest during this stage, although the intentionality of kenosis might differentiate it from more spontaneous or overwhelming dissolutions, and this stage represents the core deconstruction of the ego, a challenging period where the old self is actively coming apart.
- The subsequent Stage 3, The Point of No Return & Surrender, is a critical juncture where the dissolution process becomes overwhelming, and resistance is no longer tenable, often involving a conscious or unconscious act of surrender to the experience, which can be pivotal in navigating the subsequent stages, as described by Symbosity and Wikipedia - Taoism.
- The process of surrender is a moment of profound release, often accompanied by fear, and is characterized by a sense of inevitability, where the individual recognizes that the process cannot be stopped or controlled by the ego, as discussed by Daniel Hannah in "The Surrender" and Symbosity in Stage 2.
- This surrender marks a turning point where the most intense suffering associated with resistance begins to lessen, and is a key aspect of Category 4: The Kenotic Void, where it is often a deliberate spiritual posture, and is also a precursor to the full experience of Category 5: The Dissolving Void.

## Limitations and Pitfalls of Metaphors
- Following surrender, the individual typically enters the core ego death experience, characterized by the complete or near-complete dissolution of subjective self-identity, and often involves profound experiences of unity, boundless awareness, or an encounter with a vast void or ground of being, as described by MasterClass in Stage 2 "Ego loss" and Medium in Stage 3 "Ego Death".
- The key characteristics and phenomenological features of this stage include the complete loss of subjective self-identity, experience of unity or oneness, encounter with the void or emptiness, transcendence of time and space, pure awareness or consciousness, and a noetic quality, where the experience feels more real than ordinary reality, as discussed by Leary, Typology, and Symbosity.
- The experience can be accompanied by powerful emotions, including bliss, awe, peace, or terror, and is often described as a sense of merging with the environment, the universe, or a transcendent Other, with boundaries between self and world dissolving entirely, as described by MasterClass, Typology, and Symbosity.
- The connection to typology of abyssal encounters is also significant, where the struggle and fear preceding surrender can be extreme manifestations of Category 1: The Traumatic Void, and the act of surrender itself is central to Category 4: The Kenotic Void, and is a precursor to the full experience of Category 5: The Dissolving Void, as discussed by Typology and Symbosity.
