## Philosophical Foundations

- Friedrich Nietzsche is regarded as the herald of modern nihilism, and his pronouncement "God is dead… and we have killed him" captures the spiritual crisis of his age, where the collapse of the highest values and absolutes has led to an emerging cultural nihilism and the realization that objective meaning and morality lose their grounding.

- Nietzsche's concept of the [Übermensch](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/fa0328f1-5861-4575-bc49-840b75ecf79c) (overman) and the project of the revaluation of values were meant to overcome nihilism by affirming life in the absence of given meaning, but from a nihiltheist perspective, his solution remains within the human domain, and instead, Nihiltheism takes Nietzsche's diagnosis further by asking what if the void itself is treated as the locus of the sacred.

- The dissertation aspires to be the definitive text on Nihiltheism, a testament to how one might find transcendence in the very heart of nothingness, by charting a course that refuses consolation and facile answers, choosing instead an unsentimental mysticism of the Void.

- The concept of Nihiltheism is influenced by the philosophical ideas of [Friedrich Nietzsche](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/373f531e-23bc-470c-9ea7-cb2e7cf5d82e), who radicalized the inquiry into nihilism, and [Martin Heidegger](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/cda04228-d065-48e0-ab44-477f29883fae), who deepened the Western philosophical engagement with Nothingness, or Das Nichts, in his 1929 lecture "What Is Metaphysics?".

- Heidegger's philosophy reveals that Nothingness is not merely an absence of beings, but an active force or presence in its own right, which can be encountered through the experience of profound anxiety, or Angst, where all beings recede and the familiar world slips away, leaving one faced with the naked void.

- The insight that Nonbeing is integral to the disclosure of Being becomes a key philosophical support for Nihiltheism's notion of a "sacred nothingness", where the void is not sheer blankness, but what makes the presence of anything possible and also what undermines all particular things from beneath.

- Heidegger's later work shows an interest in Mystical theology and Eastern thought, introducing concepts like Gelassenheit, or releasement, which resonates with apophatic surrender, and providing a method for Nihiltheism to experience the groundlessness of existence directly through phenomenological bracketing and confronting Angst.

- The philosophical foundations of Nihiltheism are also influenced by [Emil Cioran](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/63a9818f-21a6-4c04-a190-4318e44a7f9b), a Romanian-French philosopher and aphorist, who provides a more literary and despairing voice, exuding a deeply nihilistic pessimism with an undercurrent of dark mysticism, and emphasizing the futility of existence and the idea that nothingness is our true home.

- Cioran's works, such as "[The Trouble with Being Born](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/15d7ac5c-07d2-4def-83d6-3a65ec8d6204)" and "[A Short History of Decay](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/d610cece-0883-4877-8b11-8955e6ef42e9)", encapsulate the nihiltheist paradox, where whether or not one believes in God, one ultimately confronts the same abyss, either the void of a godless universe or the void that is God's true nature, as expressed in his aphorism "Without God, everything is nothingness; and with God? Supreme nothingness".

- The philosopher Cioran's ideas align with Nihiltheism, which suggests that God, if the term is to be salvaged, must be re-understood as a supreme void, rather than a being among beings, and his work draws from mystics, Gnostics, and Buddhism's notion of Nirvana.

- Cioran's extreme pessimism, as reflected in his statement that "life is a failure of taste, and existence is a tragic joke," feeds into Nihiltheism by showing an unsentimental acceptance of the worst, including the absence of Providence and the incurable nature of suffering and decay.

- Cioran's work also hints at a perverse yearning for nothingness as a release, which is a key aspect of Nihiltheism, and he once asked if existence is our exile and nothingness our home, a question that Nihiltheism answers in the affirmative.

- The Christian existentialist theologian Paul Tillich's ideas form a bridge between existential despair and a reimagined concept of God that resonates with Nihiltheism, and he diagnosed modernity's malady as the "anxiety of meaninglessness" in works like The Courage to Be.

- Tillich suggested that the traditional theistic image of God often collapses under doubt, leading to an existential anxiety, but rather than retreating from this, he boldly suggested that such despair could be a gateway to a deeper understanding of the divine, which he referred to as "the God above the God of theism".

- Tillich's theology involved a kind of negation of the conventional God, and he insisted that God is not a particular entity, but rather the depth or ground in which all beings exist and have meaning, which is an idea that is quite in line with the apophatic tradition.

- For Nihiltheism, Tillich's contribution is twofold, as his analysis of meaninglessness provides a theological context for nihilism, and his idea of the "God above God" and God as groundless Ground points toward a divinity that is compatible with nothingness, suggesting that true faith is "accepting the acceptance of being accepted" by something infinite, even in the face of apparent nothingness.

- The concept of non-being and the idea of finding ultimate concern in the act of courageously affirming being despite nonbeing are discussed in relation to the philosophers Cioran and Tillich, with Tillich's idea of God as the power of being that resists nonbeing being particularly relevant.

- Tillich's philosophy is seen as an important ally in framing a view of divinity that is non-personal, non-ontic, and reached through negation of ordinary meaning, which is in line with the principles of Nihiltheism and its concept of "sacred groundlessness".

- [Peter Wessel Zapffe](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/9e5e688a-c8c8-4809-bc05-b6b76622135a), a Norwegian philosopher, is introduced as a representative of the most existentially pessimistic strand, arguing that human consciousness has over-evolved, leading to profound existential angst and a realization of the indiﬀerence and meaninglessness of the cosmos.

- Zapffe's philosophy identifies a fundamental human predicament, where humans crave justice, purpose, and a sense of cosmic significance, but the actual world gives no indication that such things are real, leading to a mismatch between human needs and indiﬀerent reality that is the root of nihilism and despair.

- Zapffe's response to this predicament is deeply pessimistic, suggesting that humans employ protective strategies to limit their consciousness of the absurd, and ultimately concluding that the kindest thing would be for humanity to cease reproducing, a conclusion that Nihiltheism diverges from by asking if there might be a way to transform the confrontation with meaninglessness into a spiritual path.

- Nihiltheism accepts Zapffe's diagnosis that the world does not supply the meaning humans hunger for, but instead of counseling a sort of resigned extinction, it asks if there might be a way to find transcendence in the absence of meaning, refusing to see the outcome as purely negative and instead seeking a way to transform the confrontation with meaninglessness into a spiritual path.

- The concept of Nihiltheism is introduced as a reinterpretation of the human search for meaning, suggesting that true fulfillment lies in embracing [emptiness](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/3a03be16-be0b-49d5-9b77-a09c6eab88b6) and the total groundlessness of existence, rather than seeking something that does not exist.

- The idea of Nihiltheism is built upon the foundations of various thinkers, including Nietzsche, Heidegger, Cioran, Tillich, and Zapffe, who each contribute to a rich palette of concepts, such as the absurdity of existence, the death of transcendental guarantees, and the presence of Nothingness at the heart of being.

## Mystical Traditions

- Zapffe's tragic philosophy is seen as a catalyst for Nihiltheism, as it brings individuals to the brink of the abyss of meaninglessness, inviting them to jump in and discover what lies on the other side, not to die, but to drown the self and find a new mode of being.

- The text highlights the trajectory of these thinkers, from Nietzsche's declaration of the void to Heidegger's exploration of the void as an existential reality, Cioran's nihilistic mysticism, Tillich's reach towards a God found through the void, and Zapffe's confrontation with the dire facts of meaninglessness.

- Nihiltheism is positioned at the crossroads of existential philosophy and mysticism, drawing on the apophatic or "[negative theology](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/375bbe46-e962-4e3b-a833-33ed0f67d5de)" tradition, which emphasizes the limitations of human concepts in describing the Divine, and instead, insists on the ultimate reality being beyond all names and concepts, a Nothingness to finite minds.

- The apophatic mysticism tradition is noted to stretch across cultures, including Christianity, Neoplatonism, Gnosticism, [Buddhism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/a6d31634-6ad7-47bc-9ede-d986f8f1229e), Hinduism, and [Daoism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/3ddfab29-0ddf-45f7-acdf-deb834fdcad8), with key figures such as Pseudo-Dionysius, [Meister Eckhart](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/1245acf1-01d7-419c-a0a2-5e96a260d84c), St. [John of the Cross](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/27403744-10b7-4947-9a7c-991113831ec8), and concepts like [Śūnyatā](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/3a03be16-be0b-49d5-9b77-a09c6eab88b6) and Advaita Vedanta's neti neti, all of which influence Nihiltheism's radicalization of apophaticism and its wedding to modern nihilism.

- The concept of Nihiltheism is influenced by the ideas of Eastern Christian mystic Pseudo-Dionysius, who wrote that God is "not soul, not intellect, not wisdom, not one, not oneness, not godhead, not goodness," and ultimately "not being… and beyond all being", describing the final approach to God as entering a "darkness beyond light" where one encounters "the unknowing that is beyond knowledge".

- Pseudo-Dionysius' method of approaching truth via radical negation is shared with Nihiltheism, but whereas he believed in a God who transcends this nothingness, Nihiltheism questions whether the abyss itself is all there is, with no further posited deity behind it.

- Meister Eckhart, a German Dominican mystic, also asserted apophatic ideas, stating that to reach God, one must let go of every concept and image of God, and that "God is not a thing among things, but is no-thing because He is beyond all things", implying that the soul must become empty to encounter the real Godhead, which is a formless emptiness and a state of divine nothingness.

- Eckhart's radical mysticism feeds into Nihiltheism's view of transcendence, where to find the divine, one must annihilate every finite meaning, become empty, and find that God is "nothing", yet this nothing is not a mere void but a plenum of super-essential reality.

- St. John of the Cross, a Spanish mystic, also influenced Nihiltheism with his concept of the [Dark Night of the Soul](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/08f69072-9971-4878-ab1a-863dd3519bb5), where the soul seeking union with God must pass through a purgative stage of unknowing and abandonment, feeling totally bereft of God's light, but ultimately preparing for a higher union beyond sensation.

- Nihiltheism finds an archetype in John's concept of the Dark Night, where the journey through desolation and meaninglessness might be the only path to a genuine encounter with what is ultimate, although Nihiltheism extends this idea to question whether the absence of God is not just a sign of God's closeness, but rather the ultimate reality itself.

- The concept of Apophatic Mysticism, specifically the Via Negativa and the Divine Darkness, is explored in relation to Nihiltheism, which holds that the darkness or nothingness does not necessarily reveal a new dawn of traditional divinity, but rather one comes to love the night itself.

- The idea of mystical negation is present in Nihiltheism, where the collapse of meaning can be a stage of growth towards a new form of insight, similar to the concept of the "dark night of the soul" in Christian mysticism, as discussed by John.

- Buddhist thought, particularly [Mahāyāna](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/88bf7cf6-596c-4e61-b628-2c2a9eaf38be) Buddhism's philosophy of Śūnyatā ([emptiness](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/3a03be16-be0b-49d5-9b77-a09c6eab88b6)), provides a parallel to Nihiltheism, where all phenomena are empty of inherent self-nature, and emptiness is not a nihilistic negation but a liberation from false attachments, as articulated by [Nāgārjuna](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/37b2b4fe-c657-4726-972a-249571579939).

- The Heart Sutra's proclamation that "Form is emptiness, emptiness is form" resonates with Nihiltheism's concept of sacred nothingness, where the absolute void and the concrete world are not separate realities but one, and certain [Zen](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/33462721-ac22-4cd1-8765-9a7d88f4bef4) Buddhist teachings, such as those of Linji, also echo the nihiltheist attitude of destroying idols and clinging to realize the truth directly.

- Suﬁ Islam's concept of fanā' – the annihilation of the self in God – also shares similarities with Nihiltheism, where the process of self-annihilation and emptiness is necessary to know ultimate reality, as expressed by Suﬁ poets like [Bayazid Bastami](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/455de9ec-5e71-4ac2-ac74-ef559c918509) and Mansur al-Hallaj.

- Overall, mystical traditions across cultures, including Christianity, [Buddhism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/a6d31634-6ad7-47bc-9ede-d986f8f1229e), and Suﬁ Islam, have described God or ultimate reality in terms of nothingness, darkness, absence, and silence, and Nihiltheism stands firmly in this apophatic lineage, treating modern nihilism as akin to a mystical insight, where the negation of everything finite and conditioned can lead to the unconditioned Nothing.

## Comparison with Other Philosophies

- The concept of Nihiltheism is distinct from mysticism, [existentialism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/5b225ea8-1691-4de6-8ff5-fbc225102853), and [absurdism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/75f09500-edf8-45a7-ab85-1543a46872d3), as it entertains the possibility that the negation of meaning is final, yet can lead to a transformation of consciousness that is functionally equivalent to a religious revelation by embracing the absolute lack of meaning.

- Nihiltheism differs from existentialism, which holds that life has no inherent meaning except what we create, as seen in the philosophies of [Jean-Paul Sartre](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/c84a5699-366a-40bb-a34e-d818850018bb) and [Albert Camus](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/5aa7f182-29a0-42e6-8225-0c5e138f3558), who emphasized human freedom and responsibility in creating meaning and value in a godless universe.

- In contrast to absurdism, which is illustrated by the image of Sisyphus forever rolling his rock, and responds to the absurdity with defiance and rebellion, Nihiltheism suggests that the answer to meaninglessness is not merely to create subjective meaning, but to undergo a radical transformation in how one relates to Being and Nothingness themselves.

- The key figures of existentialism, such as Sartre and Camus, focus on human freedom, human meaning-making, and human dignity in the face of absurdity, whereas Nihiltheism introduces a mystical dimension, which is somewhat post-humanist, and seeks a kind of transcendence in the very loss of meaning.

- Nihiltheism is characterized by a willingness to surrender to the abyss, seeking a paradoxical freedom and peace that is more profound than any created meaning, and is comfortable with apophatic silence, not having to justify existence on human terms at all, which is in contrast to existentialism's emphasis on human self-assertion and meaning-making.

- The concept of Nihiltheism can be seen as a revolt against the demand that life have ordinary meaning or human-centric meaning, and instead, it completes itself as total acceptance, which is distinct from Camus' call to perpetual revolt against the absurd.

- The key difference between existentialists/absurdists and nihiltheists lies in their response to the idea that life has no inherent meaning, with existentialists/absurdists choosing to rebel or define their own meaning, and nihiltheists embracing the [emptiness](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/3a03be16-be0b-49d5-9b77-a09c6eab88b6) and seeking a transcendent negation that yields a new kind of spiritual insight.

- Nihiltheism can be seen as a more inward and ascetic approach, similar to that of a monastic mystic, whereas [existentialism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/5b225ea8-1691-4de6-8ff5-fbc225102853), as represented by philosophers like [Camus](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/5aa7f182-29a0-42e6-8225-0c5e138f3558), is more heroic and worldly, focusing on ethics and authenticity in a material world.

- In contrast to classical existentialism, nihiltheism introduces the idea that authenticity might involve losing one's self in a greater nothingness, a concept that is more commonly associated with mystical states, as seen in the late writings of [Simone de Beauvoir](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/529de222-6e6f-4c74-b8d8-368fefa0263e) or the works of [Nikolai Berdyaev](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/984c2643-7410-415c-8c2d-92bd94204b9a).

- Nihiltheism differs from atheism in that it sacralizes the negation, treating the nothingness itself as the ultimate truth to dwell upon, rather than simply rejecting the idea of a personal God or supernatural order, and instead, it stays with the void, cultivating a reverence for the divine quality of emptiness.

- While nihiltheism agrees with atheism in denying the existence of a personal God or intrinsic cosmic meaning, it diverges from atheism by preserving the negative space for the sacred, making it closer to what has been termed "mystical atheism" or "religious atheism", as seen in the works of existentialists like [Miguel de Unamuno](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/73bf1a79-b2be-48d5-9f7b-435fa40c76fa) or mystical writers like Eckhart.

- In comparison to traditional theism, which affirms that ultimate reality is a personal God who is the source of all meaning, value, and being, nihiltheism rejects the positive content of the sacred, but preserves the negative space for it, making it a distinct philosophical stance that borrows from both atheism and theism, but ultimately stays with the void, treating nothingness as a sacred and ultimate truth.

- Nihiltheism is a philosophical perspective that may appear blasphemous or incoherent to theists, as it suggests that nothingness can be considered as a form of God, and this idea is often met with resistance from those who guarantee meaning through God's wisdom and goodness.

- The nihiltheist rejects traditional theism, which is seen as unsatisfying and often anthropomorphic, and instead, they find solace in the idea that the old God is no longer believable, a concept echoed in Friedrich Nietzsche's proclamation that "God remains dead, and we have killed him".

- Nihiltheism differs from militant anti-theism in that it does not attack religion or ridicule believers, but rather feels a melancholic sympathy for the religious impulse, and it often resonates with certain strains of orthodox theology, such as [negative theology](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/375bbe46-e962-4e3b-a833-33ed0f67d5de) or the silence of a Quaker meeting.

- The concept of Nihiltheism is "theistic" only in a ruptured or voided way, retaining the husk of the God concept, but placing an empty throne at its core, which is similar to the ideas of radical theologians like [Thomas J. J. Altizer](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/97ce1852-ac9b-4619-9543-b04dfb585c7c), who spoke of the "Godhead and the Nothing" being inseparable.

- Nihiltheism stands in an ambiguous space, saying to the atheist that there may be a truth that functions analogously to God, which is the truth of nothingness, and to the theist that what they seek as God might be found not as a presence but as an absence, which can be worshiped as "Nada", or nothing.

- In practice, Nihiltheism might manifest as a solitary, contemplative stance, with no churches, doctrines, or rituals, but rather a meditation on the void, a rigorous self-honesty, and an ethics of compassion born from shared fragility, and it may learn from mystical branches within religions, but cannot fully partake in them if they remain tied to positive belief.

## Core Concepts

- Nihiltheism is a lonely position, fitting for an age of fragmentation, but it could also be a hidden undercurrent in much of modern spirituality, as seen when believers speak of God being silent or absent, or when they focus on experience over belief, which edges toward nihiltheist sensibilities without naming them.

- The concept of Nihiltheism is distinct from conventional theism and secular atheism, and it shares some similarities with [existentialism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/5b225ea8-1691-4de6-8ff5-fbc225102853), but leans towards mystical resignation rather than humanistic assertion, finding allies in apophatic mystics and Eastern non-theistic religions.

- Nihiltheism is characterized by a double identity of being both utterly negative and somehow reverential, with the core tension of this identity being explored in the remaining chapters, starting with the analysis of its core concepts, including the fundamental idea of the Void and the practice of negation.

- The Void, also referred to as Nothingness, the Abyss, [emptiness](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/3a03be16-be0b-49d5-9b77-a09c6eab88b6), or nonbeing, is the central metaphysical and spiritual principle of Nihiltheism, functioning as the absolute Nothingness that underlies all existence and thought of existence, and is regarded with a kind of reverence or awe normally reserved for God.

- The Void in Nihiltheism is differentiated from a simple absence or negation, as it is not just the absence of particular things, but the absolute Nothingness that is the groundless ground of being, and is conceived as a void rather than a plenum of value, with this Void being "sacred" in the sense of being wholly other, beyond comprehension, and the source and sink of all meaning.

- The concept of Nothingness in Nihiltheism is not a mere negation, but a creative or transcendent nothingness, which appears paradoxical, and is similar to concepts in mathematics, physics, and mysticism, where "nothing" is not just an absence, but a reality that can be experienced, such as the zero in mathematics, the vacuum in physics, and the "cloud of unknowing" in mysticism.

- The Sacred Abyss, as referred to by Nihiltheism, is distinguished from mere absence, which is defined relative to something, whereas the Sacred Abyss is an absolute absence, not the absence of particular things, but the absence of any ultimate structure or guarantee in reality, and is treated as the "face" of truth, with the nihiltheist using radical negation as a method and insight to approach this ultimate reality.

- The concept of Nihiltheism suggests that by stripping away all layers of illusion, meaning, and comforting stories, one arrives at an infinite open emptiness, which can be terrifying or depressing for most people, but can also be seen as a space of freedom and ultimate reality.

- This idea is echoed in Eastern philosophies, such as the [Kyoto School](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/9b95dabd-a65f-48c7-956c-b6510ad3de6c) philosopher Nishitani Keiji's concept of "absolute nothingness" in his book Religion and Nothingness, where he distinguishes between nihility, or bad nothingness, and śūnyatā, or true nothingness that lets everything be meaningful in a non-ultimate way.

- Nihiltheism attempts to transform the despairing nothingness of nihilism into an embraced nothingness, or sacred [emptiness](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/3a03be16-be0b-49d5-9b77-a09c6eab88b6), by going through a process of total negation, which can lead to a reconception of reality as the manifestation of nothingness, and thereby appreciate it without delusion.

- The Void of Nihiltheism is not a nihilistic void of despair, but rather a mystical void of truth, which can be experienced as a kind of inner void, or silence in the soul, that provides a new anchor, or anti-anchor, and allows one to float in groundlessness, strangely liberated from the need for footing.

- To reach an understanding of this Void, Nihiltheism employs radical negation as a philosophical method and spiritual practice, borrowed from [apophatic theology](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/375bbe46-e962-4e3b-a833-33ed0f67d5de) and rigorous existential analysis, which involves systematically negating every proposition, every value, and every purported necessity until nothing remains unchallenged.

- This method of radical negation can lead to a growing silence and emptiness, similar to the "cloud of unknowing" described by mystics, and can be a frightening and resisted process, but ultimately allows one to experience a profound peace, akin to the peace described by mystics when surrendering to God's will, but here it is surrendering to the Void.

- The concept of Nihiltheism is based on absolute and recursive negation, which means that it demands the negation of its own statements and ideas, including the notion that "Nothingness is sacred", to prevent them from solidifying into dogma.

- This self-negation is a key feature of Nihiltheism, as it ensures that the philosophy remains dynamic and open-ended, rather than becoming a rigid doctrine or "ism" to cling to, and is similar to Nāgārjuna's dialectic, which reduces all views to emptiness and then declares that emptiness itself is empty.

- The intent and result of Nihiltheism's radical negation differ from those of radical skepticism or deconstruction, as it is undertaken with a quasi-spiritual intent to pursue a transformative encounter with the Void, rather than simply tearing everything down or ending in an intellectual stalemate.
