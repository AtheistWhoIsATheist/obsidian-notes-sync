- The quotes from these figures, such as Nietzsche's "God is dead... And we have killed him," and Saltus's description of life as "an affliction, a mishap, a calamity, and sometimes a curse," illustrate the visceral and lived experience of suffering and negativity inherent in existence.
- The ideas of these thinkers are linked to the concept of the Universal Nihilistic Event, which is a shared phenomenological experience that transcends individual backgrounds and perspectives, and is characterized by a sense of existential dread and encounter with nothingness.
- Worldly [[DarkNote/Nihilism]] is distinguished from Transcendent Nihilism by its modality, with the former being experienced primarily through the dissolution of worldly meaning, and the latter being seen as a gateway to something beyond, but both are expressions of the same Universal Nihilistic Event.
- The encounter with Worldly Nihilism is seen as a necessary precursor to engaging with the void and potentially leading to a redefined sense of the divine, and is characterized by feelings of dread and futility, but also represents a stark reality check that initiates the Nihiltheistic journey.
- The sources provide examples of how Worldly Nihilism manifests as boredom, futility, and existential vertigo in everyday life, and how it is experienced as a lived calamity, highlighting the importance of confronting the abyss of meaninglessness and the reality of existence.

And we have killed him", and Cioran's exploration of despair, all point to the same nihility and are symbolic gestures towards the abyss, highlighting the lived experience of suffering and futility.
- The recognition that these examples of Worldly [[DarkNote/Nihilism]], along with those of Transcendent Nihilism, are all pointing to the same Universal Nihilistic Event is fundamental to the Synthesis of Perspectives in Nihiltheism, which draws upon diverse "languages" or expressions of the void to argue for a more comprehensive understanding of nothingness and divinity.
- Nihiltheism's integrative approach acknowledges that while these figures use different cultural "language games", their core experience of confronting the abyss is the same, and this shared experience is the foundation for finding a redefined sense of the divine within through the void itself, rather than apart from it.
- The ability to see this shared experience through the lens of diverse examples, including those strongly rooted in Worldly Nihilism's sense of futility and groundlessness, is a strength of Nihiltheism's approach, allowing for a more comprehensive understanding of the void and its relationship to divinity.
