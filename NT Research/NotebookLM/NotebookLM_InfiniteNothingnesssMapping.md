1\. Buddhism (Madhyamaka/Yogācāra): Śūnyatā (Emptiness) In Madhyamaka and Yogācāra Buddhism, the core concept is Śūnyatā (Emptiness). It signifies the absence or lack of inherent existence (svabhāva) in all phenomena. This is not considered mere annihilation or nihilism. Nāgārjuna, a key figure, rigorously argues through logical analysis (prasaṅga) that all phenomena are empty of inherent, independent existence. Śūnyatā is understood dialectically, establishing the very possibility of conventional reality and dependent origination (pratītya-samutpāda). The famous declaration "Form is emptiness, emptiness is form" encapsulates this non-dual relationship. Emptiness is seen as the very condition that allows for the manifestation and transformation of all things. Yogācāra also views emptiness as the absence of subject-object duality.

2\. **Christian Mysticism (Apophatic Theology)**: Divine Darkness/Cloud of Unknowing Here, "nothingness" is related to the unknowability and ineffability of the Divine Essence. It is often referred to as the "Divine Darkness" or "Cloud of Unknowing". The emphasis is on negation (Via Negativa) because God transcends all finite concepts and positive descriptions. This negation is not an end in itself but a means to reach beyond conceptual grasp to a transcendent reality. The concept of Kenosis (self-emptying) is also relevant. The epistemology here is "knowing via unknowing".

3\. Sufism: Fanāʾ (Annihilation) and Baqaʾ (Subsistence) A core concept is Fanāʾ (Annihilation), described as the annihilation or passing away of the individual self or ego in the overwhelming presence of the Divine. This is not an ultimate endpoint but is typically followed by Baqaʾ (Subsistence), remaining in the presence of God or abiding in God, where the self exists in unity with the Divine attributes or reality. The "nothingness" here is the dissolution of the limited self relative to the infinite presence of God.

4\. **Kabbalah**: Ayin (Primordial Nothingness) and Tehom (Abyss) Conceptualized as Ayin (Primordial Nothingness) and Tehom (Abyss). Ayin is understood as the ultimate source before divine emanation, related to Ein Sof (the Infinite). It refers to the primordial nothingness that precedes divine emanation (the Sefirot) and the manifestation of reality. Ayin is considered a state beyond comprehension and a higher, more subtle reality than Yesh (Something or Being). Tzimtzum (Divine Contraction) is also a relevant concept.

5\. **Taoism**: Wu (Non-being/Nothing) and Wuji (Ultimate Non-Being) Key terms include Wu (Non-being/Nothing) and Wuji (Ultimate Non-Being). This is conceptualized as an undifferentiated, primordial emptiness, the ground of potentiality and the generative source from which Being (You) and the manifest world emerge. Laozi and Zhuangzi describe Wu not as mere absence but as a primordial, undifferentiated emptiness or potentiality from which all things arise. Wu Wei (Non-action) is a related principle, involving effortless alignment with the natural flow.

6\. **Existentialism (Sartre/Heidegger)**: le Néant and Das Nichts Nothingness (le Néant for Sartre, Das Nichts for Heidegger) is understood in relation to human existence.

•

Sartre sees nothingness arising from the negating capacity and freedom of consciousness (being-for-itself) when confronting inert being (being-in-itself). This encounter is the source of anguish but also the ground of freedom.

•

Heidegger views Das Nichts as fundamental and ontologically prior, that which allows beings to appear as beings, and it is revealed through the mood of profound anxiety (Angst). It is intimately linked to freedom and the confrontation with meaninglessness and the absurd. Albert Camus, also an existentialist figure, explores the confrontation with the Absurd.

7\. Advaita Vedanta: Neti Neti (Negation) and Brahman While not using "nothingness" as a core term in the same way, this tradition employs negation (Neti Neti - "not this, not this") to describe the ultimate reality, Brahman, which is non-dual Being/Consciousness. The phenomenal world (Maya) and individual ego are seen as illusory manifestations relative to this absolute Reality. Realization involves understanding the illusory nature of the separate self and the world, pointing to a fundamental non-duality (Atman is Brahman). The method of negation is used to negate all descriptions and attributes of the phenomenal world and the individual ego in order to realize Brahman which is beyond conceptual description.

8\. Neo-Platonism: The One (Beyond Being) and Apophasis The ultimate principle, The One, is considered beyond Being. It is approached epistemologically through negation (Apophasis or Via Negativa), acknowledging what cannot be said about it. This transcendent principle is approached via negation.
