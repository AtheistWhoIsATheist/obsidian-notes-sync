A distinction is made between "worldly" suicide (driven by loss or external failure) and "nihilistic" suicide (from deep, authentic engagement with meaninglessness), the latter being debated as potentially more "authentic" due to its logical consistency within a nihilistic worldview. The terror and allure of madness might be akin to encountering the divine or the "Other," leading to ultimate liberation or exploration. Society often labels those embracing Nihilism as mad or depressed, but these individuals may perceive themselves as having a more sincere grasp of reality, and this profound discomfort can be a precursor to unique wisdom or enlightenment. The "revelatory power of illness and depression" reveals the starkness of existence.

3. **Renunciation, Solitude, and Silence**: True religiosity and the path to Transcendence involve a profound dissatisfaction with the world and a radical renunciation of its superficialities. This entails recognizing the illusory nature of material concerns and worldly attachments. The dissolution of the ego or "self-life" is a critical step, leading to unity, pure consciousness, and the experience of oneness with existence, demanding one experience oneself as nothing. Idle talk and distraction are barriers to confronting Nihilism; therefore, solitude and silence are necessary conditions for developing an understanding of this experience and its language. The struggle of St. Therese of Lisieux to articulate her spiritual darkness is cited as an example of the quietist silence left by the nihilistic experience.

4. **"Spiritual Death" and Liberation**: The psychological distress associated with Nihilism forces a contrast between "natural life" (the pursuit of happiness and avoidance of suffering) and "spiritual death," where one comes to terms with the ultimate meaninglessness of such pursuits. True liberation from the "horrors of existence" comes not from attachment to worldly concerns but from a profound realization of the world's unreality.

5. **Psychedelic Journeys ("Augmented Nihilism")**: The sources propose that profound nihilistic experiences, and thus heightened psychological distress, do not solely require years of traditional study. Psychedelic journeys, particularly with psilocybin, can serve as a catalyst for "Augmented Nihilism". This is described as a direct, terrifying, yet profound confrontation with the "Other," where the Nothingness of the world is magnified to immeasurable heights, and this "Other" world is experienced as more real than the mundane. It involves an eradication of the ego, a disconnection from the worldly body, and a meeting with a "bigger something," representing a true "opening up" of the Transcendent. This experience is simultaneously deeply satisfying ("at home," "infinitely satisfied") and terrifying, often leading to paralysis and fear.

Thus, the psychological distress that Nihilism inflicts is far from a mere pathology to be suppressed. It is, in fact, the very crucible within which human consciousness is refined, sharpened, and, in a profound paradox, offered a glimpse of a different, more authentic reality, contingent upon a relentless, unsparing confrontation with the void itself.
