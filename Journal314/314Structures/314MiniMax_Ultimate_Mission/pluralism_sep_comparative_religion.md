**1. Definition and Scope of Comparative Philosophy of Religion:**

*   **Definition:** Comparative philosophy of religion is a subfield of both philosophy of religion and comparative philosophy.
    *   Philosophy of religion deals with philosophical questions related to religious belief and practice, including the concept of religion itself.
    *   Comparative philosophy compares concepts, theories, and arguments from different philosophical traditions.
*   **Meanings of "Comparative Philosophy of Religion":**
    *   The comparative philosophical study of different religions.
    *   The comparative study of different philosophies of religion. (This can be a form of metaphilosophy, investigating philosophical inquiry into religion.)
*   **Scope:**  It involves comparing ideas from two or more philosophical or religious thinkers. While it could compare ideas from the same cultural tradition, it *often* compares ideas from thinkers or schools of thought in *different* traditions.
*   **Related Terms:** Cross-cultural philosophy of religion and intercultural philosophy of religion are sometimes used.

**2. Methodological Considerations:**

*   **Descriptive vs. Normative Approaches:**  Some use the term "comparative" for descriptive/phenomenological approaches and "cross-cultural" for normative (evaluating truth-claims). However, this isn't a universal distinction.
*   **Dialogue vs. Comparison:**
    *   Some works use a dialogical form, bringing different voices into implicit dialogue, critically reflecting on similarities and differences.
    *   Gamwell describes it as "interreligious dialogue that has become critical reflection."
    *   Clayton describes it as "dialogic comparison by constructing imaginary conversations."
    *   Panikkar preferred "dialogue" to "comparison" because he felt "comparison" implied a detached, neutral standpoint. He favored the idea of an "encounter" between equals.
*   **Historical Perspective:**
    *   **Early Sources:** It has a comparative dimension from ancient times.
        *   Early doxographies (texts providing overviews of doctrines) in ancient Greece and Rome, and medieval India (Brahmanical, Buddhist, Jain traditions) are examples.
        *   They placed divergent views in juxtaposition, often in a partisan way, or to highlight similarities/differences.
        *   Examples: Cicero's *De Natura Deorum*, Clement of Alexandria's *Stromateis*. Indian examples include *Madhyamaka-hṛdaya-kārikā*, *Ṣaḍ-darśana-samuccaya*, and *Sarva-siddhānta-saṃgraha*.
    *   **The Dialogue Form:** Dialogues juxtapose different viewpoints.
        *   Examples: *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad*, *Dīgha Nikāya*, *Mahābhārata*.
        *   Distinction between didactic dialogues (showing superiority of one position) and contemplative/impartial dialogues.
        *   Cicero's *De Natura Deorum* influenced Hume's *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion*.
    *   **Modern European Comparative Philosophy of Religion:**  David Tracy identifies David Hume, Immanuel Kant, and G. W. F. Hegel as key figures in the 18th and 19th centuries who pursued a comparative approach.
        *   **Hume:** Examined origins of religion in human nature (hopes/fears), and used an evolutionary theory. *Dialogues* examined arguments for and against belief in God.
        *   **Kant:** Interested in comparative issues (lectures on Eastern religions, etc.), but his main concern was a philosophical elucidation of the Christian religion.
        *   **Hegel:**  Devised a typology of "Determinate Religion" in his *Lectures on the Philosophy of Religion*, comparing Buddhism, Hinduism, Judaism, etc., but at times he ignored historical context, internal developments, and living religions.
    *   **Twentieth- and Twenty-First-Century Developments:** Key figures include Nishida Kitarō, Sri Aurobindo, Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan, Wilfred Cantwell Smith, Raimundo Panikkar, John Hick, Ninian Smart, Keith Ward, Robert Cummings Neville, and Arvind Sharma.
        *   **Nishida:** Developed a philosophy of religion using Christian and Buddhist ideas, focusing on "absolute nothingness" and the "absolute present."
        *   **Sri Aurobindo:** Integrated Western and Asian thought, developed "integral Yoga," and saw philosophy as spiritual discipline.
        *   **Radhakrishnan:**  His view of religion is comparable to Hick's religious pluralism.  He sometimes gave pride of place to Advaita Vedānta.
        *   **Ninian Smart:** Comparative from the outset, with a phenomenological method.  He promoted the study of worldviews and multidimensional analysis of the "sacred."
    *   **Events, Projects, Journals, and Book Series:**  The article lists several conferences, journals and book series that made significant contributions to the field.

**3. Synthesizing vs. Heterogenizing Approaches**

*   **Synthesizing Approaches:**  Emphasize similarities between religious ideas.
    *   **Arthur Schopenhauer:** Drew on Platonism, Kantian idealism, Indian Vedānta, and Buddhism.
    *   **Perennial Philosophy:** (Huxley) Multiple philosophies/religions share a common core of doctrines grounded in a transcultural experience.  Requires comparative inquiry. (Huxley quoted from Christian mystics to Buddhist texts.)
    *   **Religious Pluralism:** (Hick)  More than one religion is true and salvific.  Debates between exclusivists, inclusivists, and pluralists involve comparison.
    *   **Problem-Solving Approaches:** (Nagasawa)  Focus on shared philosophical problems, combining resources from different traditions.

*   **Heterogenizing Approaches:** Emphasize dissimilarities.
    *   **Internalist Pluralism:** (Harrison)  Meaningful discussion of "religious realities" only occurs within specific "conceptual schemes" or "faith-stances."  Apparent disagreements are often merely differences.
    *   **Radical Pluralism:** (Phillips, Burley) Focus on understanding religious and nonreligious perspectives in their particularities, without seeking a general theory about religion. Burley emphasizes description, which can serve a critical function.  Ramal:  Underlying shared humanity can be found beneath diverse expressions of religiosity.

**4. Examples of Comparative Studies (Illustrative):**

*   **Personal Identity, the Self, and Death:**  Hick's *Death and Eternal Life* (Christianity, Hinduism, Buddhism).
*   **Comparative Religious Ethics:** Stalnaker (Xunzi and Augustine), Yearley (Mencius and Aquinas), Walden (Zhu Xi, Schopenhauer, Śāntideva, Nishida Kitarō), Ok-sun An (early Buddhism and Confucianism).
*   **Religion and (or beyond) Rationality:** Clayton (Hume and Rāmānuja), Carr and Ivanhoe (Zhuangzi and Kierkegaard).
*   **Concepts of Belief, Knowledge, and Witchcraft:** Hallen and Sodipo (Yorùbá epistemology).

**5. Challenges:**

*   **The Eurocentric Legacy:** Western categories (God, creation, sin, etc.) may be imposed on other cultures.  There is a call for reform and revision.  Some are calling for "decolonization".
*   **Doing Violence to the Integrity of Traditions?:**  The problem-solving approach might fail to respect the integrity of traditions by borrowing ideas without the proper context.  Avoid this by giving due attention to cultural context. Also consider the idea of collaboration in which resources are shared across traditions.
*   **The Entanglements of Traditions:** Traditions have undergone mutual influence throughout history. A balance is needed to avoid treating them as totally separate, while also recognizing that they are not all the same.
*   **The Difficulty of Acquiring Expertise in Multiple Traditions:** Hard to gain enough expertise in more than one religion/tradition to do justice to the subject. Collective effort needed.
