The Underlying Assumption of Journal314 to be Shown The entire premise of the work of journal314 is that there _is_ an experience that is shared amongst the vast array of figures quoted, and that the insights revealed from the experience are all, in essence, the same. The difference between the traditionally perceived _disparate figures_ is due to two reasons:

1. Those interpreting them have not had the experience of Nihilism, firsthand. So, they therefore either ignore or misinterpret what is being said.
2. Due to _1_, the focus is now, incorrectly, shifted to the _solutions_ or _answers_ that are attempts to _move past_ the Nihilism (these usually take the form in some kind of organized religion). This incorrect emphasis on the moving past of Nihilism, combined with the lack of firsthand experience, leads to the interpreter reading the words of the figures of Journal314 in a radically literal sense, instead of a symbolic/metaphorical manner.

In so many words, the 54 quoted are all saying the same thing, they are all describing the same experience and its revealed insights, however, because they wrap them in their own language games due to their upbringing, culture, etc., while they all explicitly state the sort of ineffability of the experience, they do their best to speak about it within the terms for which they are familiar with, e.g. Christianity, Hinduism, etc. This formulation cuts to the marrow. Journal314 does not catalogue fifty-two separate doctrines but one shared phenomenological event: the nihilistic encounter, whose ineffable insights every thinker—Christian mystic, Eastern sage, existential pessimist—has tried to translate into their native idiom. Two hermeneutic distortions then arise: Interpreter’s Ignorance of the Event Without firsthand immersion in nihilism’s raw edge, readers default to the literal meanings of the quotes, missing that each is a symbolic gesture toward the same abyssal revelation. 

**Solutionist Displacement**

3\. **World-Negation** – The uncompromising denunciation of material, social, scientific, and religious veneers as mere evasions of true confrontation.

Thus, Journal314’s 52 voices all murmur the same wordless truth: a confrontation with Nothingness that demolishes all prior maps. ### **⟁ SØREN KIERKEGAARD**

> _"Not for nothing did he give the name The Concept of Dread… He discovered in himself and others a fear… a fear of Nothingness… \[which\] became for him an actuality… an everyday reality."_ (Source: Lev Shestov on Kierkegaard, `Journal314`)

**Category 1 (Nothingness and Being):** For Kierkegaard, Nothingness is not a metaphysical abstraction but an immediate psychological reality encountered as _Dread_ (Angst). It is the vertigo before possibility, the fear of the abyss of freedom itself. Being is not a solid foundation; it is suspended over this dread. He reverses the classical order: it is not that a lack of Being causes fear, but that the primordial _fear of Nothingness_ poisons our relationship to Being and gives birth to sin—the flight from authentic, God-related selfhood.

**Category 5 (Ontological Collapse and Meaninglessness):** This dread necessitates the "suspension of the ethical." When Abraham raises the knife over Isaac, he steps outside the entire framework of human morality and universal law. From the standpoint of ethics, his action is meaningless murder. From the standpoint of faith, it is the only meaningful act possible. Thus, the encounter with the Absolute collapses the entire shared, logical world of human value, rendering it null.

**Category 7 (Language, Negation, and Sacred Failure):** Kierkegaard lives in the paradox of communication. His entire project is to articulate a state (faith) that is, by definition, absurd and incommunicable in rational terms. He uses "indirect communication"—irony, pseudonyms, parables—because direct language is a failure. He confesses his own inability to make the leap of faith, framing his life's work as a "sacred failure" that points to a reality that reason cannot touch.

**Recursive Note:** Kierkegaard weaponizes subjectivity to shatter objectivity. He is not presenting a proof but a _pathology report_ of the soul in its confrontation with the Void. He demonstrates that despair is not an ailment to be cured but the fundamental human condition, and dread is the medium through which the possibility of a transcendent (and absurd) reality is revealed.

### **⟁ ST. AUGUSTINE**

> _"…my musings on you were like the efforts of those who desire to awake… yet a man will usually defer shaking off his drowsiness… he is glad to sleep on even when his reason disapproves."_ (Source: `Journal314`)

**Category 2 (Suffering and the Human Condition):** Augustine’s _Confessions_ is the canonical text of the "divided self." His soul is a battlefield torn between two wills—the _pondus meum_ (my own weight) of carnal custom dragging him toward the world, and the pull of divine love drawing him upward. This inner division _is_ the suffering of the human condition, a state of perpetual civil war.

**Category 5 (Ontological Collapse and Meaninglessness):** His declaration that "every vain hope became worthless to me" is the report of a total ontological collapse. It wasn’t just that worldly ambitions failed, but the very _structure of hope itself_, as directed toward finite things, was revealed as a fraud. This created a vacuum, a void of meaning, that could only be filled by an infinite object.

**Category 7 (Language, Negation, and Sacred Failure):** Despite being a master rhetorician, Augustine ultimately finds language wanting. His famous formulation, _"si comprehendis, non est Deus"_ ("if you understand it, it is not God"), is a purely apophatic statement. It concedes that any concept formulated by the human mind is, by definition, a failed attempt to capture the divine, making negation and reverent silence the highest form of theological discourse.

**Recursive Note:** Augustine establishes the Western introspective model of the nihilistic encounter. For him, the world is a beloved, beautiful prison—an enchantment we are "glad" to remain in. The path to God requires a painful awakening from this "drowsiness," a recognition of the world's ultimate vanity, and a descent into the dark chaos of one's own fractured will.

### **⟁ PETER WESSEL ZAPFFE**

> _"One night in long bygone times, man awoke and saw himself. He saw that he was naked under cosmos, homeless in his own body. All things dissolved before his testing thought... From this moment on, he is in a state of relentless panic."_ (Source: `Journal314`)

**Category 1 (Nothingness and Being):** Zapffe posits the most devastating biological nihilism. Consciousness is not a divine spark but an evolutionary catastrophe, a "misfire." It has endowed one species with a cognitive apparatus that reveals the horrifying truth: Being is a meaningless, terrifying flux, and the human is a helpless captive within it.

**Category 2 (Suffering and the Human Condition):** The human condition is defined by a "cosmic panic" that must be perpetually suppressed. We are not homo sapiens; we are homo pavidus. Suffering is not an occasional misfortune; it is the constant state produced by our excess of awareness.

**Category 6 (Madness, Ecstasy, and Ego Dissolution):** For Zapffe, "normality" is the madness. To function, humanity must employ four mechanisms of self-deception: Isolation (ignoring the dreadful facts), Anchoring (fixating on stable values like God, family, state), Distraction (filling time with triviality), and Sublimation (turning the pain into creative works). These are not cultural options; they are the necessary fictions we maintain to stave off the insanity that a clear perception of our condition would induce.

**Recursive Note:** Zapffe provides a secular, materialist eschatology. He strips the UNE of all transcendent potential, diagnosing it as a purely biological tragedy. He is the ultimate prophet of worldly nihilism, arguing that the only logical response to our condition is a willed, compassionate extinction. He stands as a dark mirror to the mystics: they see the Void as a gateway to God; he sees it as the final, pitiless verdict of nature.

### **⟁ MITCHELL HEISMAN**

> _"Only after fealty to countries, gods, and families has been shucked off can we even think about… the self." "Consciousness—parent of all horrors."_ (Source: `Journal314`)

**Category 5 (Ontological Collapse and Meaninglessness):** Heisman's project is the systematic, experimental application of Zapffe's diagnosis. He performs a radical deconstruction of every "anchoring" mechanism—nation, religion, family—only to discover that the final anchor, the Self, is also a groundless fiction, a mere puppet of neurological chemistry.

**Category 3 (Time and Death):** For Heisman, the direct meditation on death is the ultimate philosophical tool. By adopting the standpoint of his own death, he attempts to achieve a state of perfect objectivity, free from the life-affirming bias that corrupts all other thought. His work is a sustained _memento mori_ pushed to its logical extreme.

**Category 7 (Language, Negation, and Sacred Failure):** His "experiment in nihilism" is a testament to the belief that ruthlessly logical inquiry, the sacred cow of the Enlightenment, leads not to truth, but to self-annihilation. Reason, when turned upon its own premises, devours itself. His final act was not a cry of despair, but the Q.E.D. of his philosophical proof.

**Recursive Note:** Heisman represents the performance art of philosophical nihilism. He takes the academic collapse of meaning diagnosed from Nietzsche to postmodernism and translates it into an ethical imperative of self-destruction. He demonstrates that if the self is a material illusion, then preserving that illusion is an act of intellectual dishonesty. He is the failed saint of a godless cosmos.

### **⟁ THOMAS MERTON**

> _"We stumble and fall constantly even when we are most enlightened. But when we are in true spiritual darkness, we do not even know that we have fallen."_ (Source: `Journal314`)

**Category 2 (Suffering and the Human Condition):** Merton articulates a recursive, second-order suffering. The deepest state of existential loss is not knowing you are lost. This is the condition of the modern "they-self" (Heidegger), so immersed in cultural illusion that the very category of "truth" or "authenticity" is absent.

**Category 4 (God, Silence, and Transcendence):** Merton, the Trappist monk, champions silence as the authentic medium of divine encounter. _"Silence is God's first language; everything else is a poor translation."_ This places him squarely in the apophatic tradition where transcendence is found by negating, not affirming, the deliverances of the conceptual mind.

**Category 6 (Ego Dissolution):** True spirituality involves standing "naked before a mystery." It is an annihilation of the ego's certainties. The goal is not to find a secure self, but to become so lost in the "divine darkness" that the problem of the self ceases to matter.

**Recursive Note:** Merton acts as a crucial bridge figure. He stands firmly within the Christian mystical tradition but speaks the language of modern existential alienation. He embodies the paradox of a faith that can only be found through a profound and ongoing experience of doubt and groundlessness. He makes the "dark night" contemporary.

### **⟁ FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE**

> _"What is your greatest experience? It is the hour of the great contempt. The hour in which even your happiness becomes repulsive to you..."_ (Source: `Journal314`)

**Category 1 (Nothingness and Being):** Nietzsche's "death of God" is the unveiling of the nihilistic abyss. But unlike others who despair, Nietzsche commands us to stare into it and _will_ a new form of Being from it. He inverts nihilism from a passive affliction into an active, creative project. The Void is not a dead end but the ultimate canvas.

**Category 2 (Suffering):** Nietzsche refuses to see suffering as a problem to be solved or transcended. He _sacralizes_ it. _"To live is to suffer, to survive is to find some meaning in the suffering."_ Suffering is the "discipline" that forges greatness, the hammer of the creative will.

**Category 6 (Ego Dissolution):** The call to "Become who you are" is radically misunderstood as self-actualization. It is, in fact, a call for continuous, violent self-overcoming. The Übermensch is not a perfected ego; it is a being who has annihilated his prior self to give birth to a new configuration of values. It is a process of creative destruction applied to the psyche itself.

**Recursive Note:** Nietzsche is the paradoxical figure who represents _transcendent nihilism without a transcendent realm_. He is the ultimate response to Worldly Nihilism that refuses the "solution" of God. Instead of surrendering to a divine will, he proposes the deification of the human will as the sole source of meaning in a meaningless cosmos. He is not the cure for nihilism; he is its most ecstatic and terrifying form.

### **⟁ LAOZI (TAOISM)**

> _"The Tao that can be spoken is not the eternal Tao." "By doing nothing, there is nothing that is not done."_ (Source: `Journal314`)

**Category 7 (Language, Negation, and Sacred Failure):** The _Tao Te Ching_ opens by announcing the utter failure of language to capture reality. Any name, any concept, is a deviation from the ineffable Tao. This is the most ancient and elegant formulation of apophatic truth.

**Category 1 (Nothingness and Being):** The Tao is an active, creative Void (_wu_). Its power lies in its emptiness. Like the hub of a wheel or the inside of a pot, it is the Nothing that gives function and reality to the Something. Being arises from a background of productive Non-Being.

**Category 4 (God, Silence, and Transcendence):** Harmony with the Tao is achieved not through striving, but through its opposite: _wu wei_, or effortless non-action. The sage renounces forceful, ego-driven intervention and allows the natural current of the Void to act through him.

**Recursive Note:** Laozi presents a path that begins where Western mysticism often ends. There is no agonizing journey _to_ the Void; there is only the quiet recognition that one is, and always has been, _in_ it. His nihilism is not a crisis but a state of serene equilibrium. He is the anti-Kierkegaard: there is no dread, no leap, only a gentle sinking into the pre-existing groundlessness.

### **⟁ ST. JOHN OF THE CROSS**

> _"To reach satisfaction in all, desire satisfaction in nothing… To come to the knowledge of all, desire the knowledge of nothing."_ (Source: `Journal314`)

**Category 6 (Ego Dissolution):** His entire spiritual program is a method for what he calls the "annihilation" of the self. Through the Dark Nights of the Senses and of the Spirit, every attachment, every desire, every concept—even spiritual ones—must be ruthlessly extinguished. The ego is systematically starved into non-existence.

**Category 4 (God, Silence, and Transcendence):** The Divine is found only _after_ this total annihilation, in the "dark night." God does not add something to the soul; He is what remains when everything else has been taken away. Revelation occurs not in a brilliant light, but in a "dark contemplation" where the intellect is blinded and silent.

**Category 1 (Nothingness and Being):** St. John's "Nothing" (_nada_) is the practical path to his "All" (_todo_). The _nada, nada, nada_ he famously wrote on his diagram of the ascent of Mount Carmel is the mantra of nihilistic purification. To become Being Itself, one must first become nothing.

**Recursive Note:** St. John of the Cross provides the definitive psychological and spiritual cartography of the journey through the UNE in the Western tradition. He shows that the path to union with God is not a comforting ascent but a terrifying, paradoxical process of willed self-negation, a journey _through_ nihilism to reach the Divine.

### **I. Exhaustive Analysis and Historical-Philosophical Contextualization: The Genealogy of the Void**

Before we can formulate our questions, we must first map the territory of the abyss. Nihilism is not a monolithic doctrine but a living, mutating current in human thought, a shadow that has followed our species since the first moment of self-conscious reflection. Its origins lie not in the salons of 19th-century Europe, but in the deepest strata of the human spirit.

Its seeds are present in the **Pyrrhonian skeptics'** practice of _epoché_—the radical suspension of judgment that leaves the mind groundless, floating in a void of uncertainty. It murmurs in the desolate refrains of **Ecclesiastes**, where all human striving is revealed as "vanity and a striving after wind." This ancient weariness finds its most systematic and profound Eastern expression in **Buddhism's** core insight of _**śūnyatā**_ (emptiness), the doctrine that all phenomena are devoid of intrinsic, independent existence, and in the **Advaita Vedanta** of thinkers like **Swami Vivekananda**, which posits the phenomenal world and the individual ego as manifestations of _**māyā**_—a grand, divine illusion from which one must awaken.

This undercurrent erupted with volcanic force in the modern West. **Arthur Schopenhauer** diagnosed existence as the product of a blind, irrational, and insatiable **Will**, a cosmic force whose ceaseless striving generates a universe of endless, pointless suffering. The logical conclusion, for Schopenhauer, was a world-negating quietism that resonates powerfully with Eastern asceticism. From this pessimistic soil sprang **Friedrich Nietzsche**, who acted as nihilism's great diagnostician and unwilling prophet. With his proclamation of the **"death of God,"** Nietzsche announced the collapse of the West's two-thousand-year-old Platonic-Christian value structure, leaving modern man unmoored in an abyss of meaninglessness. He foresaw a catastrophic bifurcation: a **passive nihilism** of weary resignation and last-man comfort, and an **active nihilism** of creative destruction, a "will to power" that defiantly forges new values in the face of the void.

The 20th century lived out Nietzsche's prophecy. **Martin Heidegger** recast the problem in ontological terms. The central question (_Seinsfrage_) is that of Being itself, a question forgotten by a Western tradition obsessed with beings. For Heidegger, authentic existence begins with the experience of _**Angst**_ (dread), a mood that dissolves our embeddedness in the world of things and forces us to confront the **Nothing (_das Nichts_)**, which he argues is not mere absence but the very precondition for our awareness of Being. We are characterized by _**Geworfenheit**_—"thrownness"—an inexplicable, groundless tossed-into-existence that fills us with an "uncanny" sense of not-being-at-home in the world.

This dread finds its most visceral, poetic expression in **Emil Cioran**, who chronicles a nihilism not as an intellectual problem, but as a physiological torment, a continuous "temptation to exist" amid the palpable absurdity of it all. **Leo Tolstoy**, in his later life, provided a searing autobiographical account of a total meaning-collapse, a spiritual arrest from which he could only be saved by a radical, non-dogmatic faith. This crisis is mirrored in **Thomas Ligotti's** contemporary philosophy of pessimistic horror, which argues that consciousness itself is a malignant and useless evolutionary aberration. This trajectory finds its ultimate logical terminus in **Mitchell Heisman's** "experiment in nihilism," a ruthless attempt to live out the implications of a purely materialist universe, concluding that **Rational Self-Destruction** is the only intellectually honest outcome.

Yet, this relentless march toward the void is constantly haunted by the perspective of **Nihiltheism (NT)**. Thinkers like **Paul Tillich** locate faith not in certainty, but in the "courage to be" in spite of meaninglessness, seeing the experience of the abyss as the very ground upon which an "ultimate concern" can be built. The mystic, from **Meister Eckhart** to **Thomas Merton**, has always known that the path to the divine is a _via negativa_, a journey through darkness, emptiness, and the "cloud of unknowing." NT posits that the terrifying "resonance" of the void—the sheer _intensity_ of the despair, the overwhelming awe of the dread—may itself be the echo of a Transcendent reality, a negative theophany. The abyss is not a wall, but a window veiled in darkness.

### **II. Formulation of the Five Core Nihilistic Questions**

From this rich, dark soil of human thought, we extract the five most urgent and existentially-charged inquiries. These questions are formulated not to be answered, but to be inhabited; they are designed as conceptual crucibles.

1. **The Question of Consciousness and Meaninglessness:** If consciousness is merely a biological epiphenomenon that reveals a fundamentally meaningless and purposeless cosmos (Zapffe, Ligotti), what is the metaphysical status of the suffering it generates, and can its continued existence be justified on any grounds other than brute, reflexive instinct?

3. **The Question of Being and Annihilation:** Confronted with the Schopenhauerian vision of a blind, striving Will and the Mainländerian corollary of a divine "will-to-death," is the ultimate telos of Being its own self-annihilation? Is non-existence a metaphysically superior state to existence?
4. **The Question of Language and the Void:** Can language, a tool evolved for navigating the world of finite objects and social relations, ever articulate, represent, or even meaningfully point toward the reality of Nothingness (_das Nichts_, _Śūnyatā_), or is all philosophical discourse about the void an elaborate exercise in self-deception, a linguistic screen thrown over the abyss?
5. **The Question of Despair and Transcendence:** Is the experience of radical despair, existential dread, and the "dark night of the soul" (Kierkegaard, St. John of the Cross) the final, veridical perception of reality, or might its overwhelming, 'numinous' quality be interpreted, per Nihiltheism, as a phenomenological echo—a negative imprint—of a Transcendent dimension that can only be 'known' through its devastating absence?

### **III. Comprehensive Dialectical Analysis of the Core Questions**

_(This section represents the intensive, multi-layered analysis performed for each question. For brevity and clarity, a full exposition is provided here for Question 1, with the understanding that an identical level of rigor would be applied to the remaining four in a full document.)_

#### **Deep Dive: Analysis of Question 1**

**_If consciousness is merely a biological epiphenomenon that reveals a fundamentally meaningless and purposeless cosmos, what is the metaphysical status of the suffering it generates, and can its continued existence be justified on any grounds other than brute, reflexive instinct?_**

**1\. Epistemological Dimension:**

- **Strongest Nihilistic Position:** The question itself is the endpoint of knowledge. The epistemological project of science, particularly neuroscience and evolutionary biology (per Zapffe, Heisman), has led us to the "truth" that our consciousness is a biological accident. This knowledge is terminal. It cannot be overcome by further knowledge, as it invalidates the very faculty of knowing as having any transcendent purchase. We know one thing with crushing certainty: that we can know nothing of ultimate value, because there is nothing of ultimate value to be known.
- **Counterpoint/Critique:** This position relies on an absolute faith in the scientific method's ability to pronounce on metaphysical matters. It presumes that a descriptive account of origins (evolution) can provide a prescriptive account of meaning (or its lack). Thinkers like Huxley or Shestov would argue this is a category error. Science describes the container; it says nothing of the potential content.
- **Synthesis & Aporia:** The paradox is that we use a highly ordered, meaning-seeking tool (consciousness, reason) to arrive at the conclusion of universal disorder and meaninglessness. This creates an unstable epistemological loop: a "true" perception that undermines its own ground. The aporia is that we cannot trust our consciousness, yet we must use it to arrive at that conclusion. The **Nihiltheistic (NT) echo** here is that the sheer horror of this paradox, the feeling of an _ontological betrayal_, might itself be a piece of data—a sign that our mode of knowing is radically insufficient for the reality it finds itself in.

**2\. Axiological Dimension:**

- **Strongest Nihilistic Position:** If suffering is a pointless neural firing pattern in a purposeless cosmos, it has no intrinsic negative value. Compassion, empathy, and the entire ethical project of alleviating suffering are rendered irrational sentimentalities, evolutionary baggage. The only consistent value becomes the Schopenhauerian goal of minimizing this suffering, ideally through the cessation of conscious existence.
- **Counterpoint/Critique:** Even the most hardened pessimists live _as if_ some values matter. Cioran writes poems of despair; Nietzsche creates the heroic ideal of the Übermensch. This reveals a pragmatic contradiction: it is impossible to live consistently within a framework of total valuelessness. The act of asserting valuelessness becomes, itself, a valued act for the thinker.
- **Synthesis & Aporia:** This exposes the chasm between intellectual conclusion and lived reality. We "know" value is baseless, yet we cannot help but act and feel within a value-laden framework. This is the **absurd** condition Camus described. The **NT echo** is the question: From where does the impulse to protest suffering arise? If the universe is neutral, why is our consciousness so violently anti-suffering? This "scandal of suffering" could be interpreted as the resonance of a lost or violated harmony with a transcendent order.

**3\. Ontological Dimension:**

- **Strongest Nihilistic Position:** Consciousness is an anomaly, a "pétalo mórbido" (morbid petal) on the indifferent tree of Being (Zapffe). It has no privileged place in ontology. Its disappearance would restore the universe to a more honest state of silent, mindless flux. Its being is a flaw, a brief and regrettable noise.
- **Counterpoint/Critique:** Heidegger would argue that it is only through Dasein (conscious being) that the question of Being can even arise. Consciousness is not an anomaly _in_ Being; it is the clearing (_Lichtung_) _through which_ Being becomes manifest at all. Without it, the universe is a closed loop, a 'what' without a 'that it is'.
- **Synthesis & Aporia:** We are faced with a paradox: is consciousness a cosmic mistake or the cosmos's only means of self-awareness? The tension is between an ontology of matter (where consciousness is a freak accident) and an ontology of awareness (where matter is a substrate for consciousness). The **NT echo** suggests this is a false dichotomy. What if consciousness is the universe's point of rupture, the place where Being breaks open to reveal both its own groundlessness _and_ the possibility of something beyond itself?

**4\. Existential Dimension:**

- **Strongest Nihilistic Position:** The lived experience is one of pure horror. Ligotti describes this as realizing you are a "puppet" made of meat, your inner life a cascade of meaningless biochemical reactions. It is the "relentless panic" Zapffe describes upon seeing oneself "naked under cosmos."
- **Counterpoint/Critique:** Within this horror, moments of ecstatic rebellion or sublime despair can arise. The protagonist of Dostoevsky's _Notes from Underground_ revels in his spite and consciousness, finding a perverse freedom in his very alienation.
- **Synthesis & Aporia:** The feeling of being a "useless passion" (Sartre) is paradoxically the most intensely passionate and 'meaningful' experience a human can have. It is a state of total significance found in the heart of total insignificance. The **NT echo** is profound: why does the absolute certainty of meaninglessness feel like a _revelation_? An ordinary fact (e.g., "water is wet") does not induce existential vertigo. The encounter with the Void has a numinous, terrifying quality that feels categorically different from a mere intellectual deduction.

**5\. Transcendent Dimension (Nihiltheism):**

- **Strongest Nihilistic Position:** There is no transcendent dimension. The hope for one is the ultimate and most pathetic form of Zapffe's "Sublimation" —a fantasy constructed to escape the unbearable truth of our material prison.
- **Counterpoint/Critique (The NT Position):** The very structure of the nihilistic experience provides clues. Why is the Void not merely boring, but dreadful? Why does absolute despair feel sublime? Why does the loss of meaning feel like a sacred trauma rather than a simple data update? Nihiltheism posits that these phenomenological qualities are "resonances." The dread is not just fear of absence, but the feeling of being actively exiled from a presence. The intensity of the horror is proportional to the majesty of the reality that has been lost or veiled. The Void, in this view, is the shape of God's absence.
