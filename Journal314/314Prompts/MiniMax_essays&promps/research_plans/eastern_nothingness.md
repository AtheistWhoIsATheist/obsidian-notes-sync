# Nothingness and Emptiness across Eastern Traditions: A Comparative Philosophical Inquiry

## Executive Overview: Framing Nothingness and Emptiness

Across South, East, and Inner Asian traditions, “nothingness” and “emptiness” name not a simple negation or absence, but a strategically ambiguous aperture through which wisdom is cultivated and liberation glimpsed. In Sanskrit, śūnya (zero, void) and its derived quality noun śūnyatā (emptiness) saturate Buddhist discourse, from early Buddhist idioms of “void” (suññatā) to the Mahāyāna Prajñāpāramitā corpus and Nāgārjuna’s non-foundational Middle Way. In Chinese Daoism, wu (non-being) is generative; you (being) is its dependent expression. The classical texts of Laozi and Zhuangzi treat emptiness as a functional principle of action, not as metaphysical negation, by articulating wu-wei (non-coercive action) and ziran (spontaneity). In Zen, mu (“no,” “nothing”) condenses a radical negation that cuts through the dualisms of being and non-being, disclosing a “bottomless ground” in which practice (zazen) and realization (kenshō/satori) coincide. In Tibetan Buddhism, stong pa nyid (voidness) is the central negation of intrinsic existence (svabhāva), now elaborated through the two truths and school-specific currents—Gelug rangtong (self-empty) versus Nyingma/Kagyu shentong (other-empty)—and through the mind-centered immediacies of Mahāmudrā and Dzogchen.

This report advances a comparative yet non-reductive narrative: emptiness/nothingness functions as a therapeutic tool, an ontological thesis, a phenomenological lens, and a practical discipline, often all at once. To navigate this complexity, the report proceeds in four movements. First, it grounds Sanskrit semantics (śūnya/śūnyatā) as both linguistic root and conceptual bridge to mathematical zero. Second, it reconstructs Madhyamaka’s sūnyatā—dependent origination, the two truths, and the Middle Way—alongside Zen’s praxis-centered articulation of mu, koans, and zazen. Third, it situates Daoist wu/you, wu-wei, and ziran as a functional discourse on emptiness-in-action. Fourth, it follows Tibetan developments: Prasaṅgika dialectic, Gelug–Nyingma divergences on the object of refutation, and Mahāmudrā/Dzogchen as gnostic totalities of empty-luminous mind. The report closes with a systematic comparison across traditions, strategic implications for practice, and an agenda for future scholarship.

The method is text-first and integrative: it reads canonical anchors (Mūlamadhyamakakārikā, Madhyamakāvatāra, Daodejing, Zhuangzi, Lotus Sūtra) through reputable encyclopedic syntheses and scholarly studies, and it keeps the typology of “emptiness” plural—semantic, meditative, logical, ontological, phenomenological—so that similarities and differences appear in clear relief rather than collapsing into a generic “void.”[^11][^3]

## Foundations in Sanskrit: śūnya (Zero), the Void in Upaniṣads, and Early Buddhist Emptiness

The semantic field of śūnya—empty, void, zero—anchors later philosophical developments. In India, śūnya is both a mathematical zero and a philosophical operator. Its noun form śūnyatā becomes a technical term across Buddhist literatures. Drawing on standard summaries and historical notes, we can distinguish at least three early tracks: (1) mathematical and semantic genealogy, (2) Upaniṣadic resonances of “void” as ultimate ground discourse (with caution), and (3) early Buddhist deployments of suññatā in both meditative andondoctrinal registers.[^11][^20][^18]

### 3.1 Etymology and Mathematical Zero

Sanskrit śūnya, “empty, void, zero,” generates śūnyatā (emptiness). Historically, the concept of zero migrated from India through the Arabic world (sifr) into European mathematics. Comparative studies emphasize that śūnya was understood not as “mere nothing” but as a sign for “emptiness” that grants positional value and organizes computation; similarly, philosophical “emptiness” functions as a relational operator that unseats essentialist claims rather than denying phenomena outright. Interpretations of śūnya’s semantic range thus oscillate between “void” and “fullness,” a tension that will echo across Buddhist, Daoist, and later Tibetan discourses.[^11][^20]

### 3.2 Upaniṣadic “Kha” and Brahmanic Emptiness

The Upaniṣads are often invoked in discussions of “void” (śūnya), sometimes via the term kha (space/emptiness) linked to Brahman. However, primary verse-level documentation for śūnya/śūnyatā across the Upaniṣads remains uneven in the present context. What can be said carefully is that “void” idioms in Brahmanical sources tend to frame an ultimate ground (Brahman) beyond qualities and predicates, distinct from Buddhist emptiness which negates intrinsic nature (svabhāva) rather than affirming a positive absolute. The prudent comparative move is therefore to treat claims about śūnya in Upaniṣads as a horizon that requires more source-critical grounding before drawing strong equivalences (information gap).[^11]

### 3.3 Early Buddhist Suññatā

Early Buddhist sources use suññatā (emptiness) both as a meditative state and as a doctrinal analysis: “the world is empty of a self or of anything pertaining to a self.” Pali texts and parallel Āgamas develop a graduated phenomenology of emptiness in concentration practice, often alongside the “signless” (animitta) and “undirected” (appaniḥita) doors to liberation. Theravāda exegesis (e.g., the Visuddhimagga) frames emptiness as “not-self” revealed by breaking the “compactness” of consolidated phenomena. Importantly, in these contexts emptiness names a mode of perception—“empty of disturbance”—rather than an ontological pronouncement on the nullity of the world.[^11]

By distinguishing these strands, we set the stage for Madhyamaka’s innovation: the identity of emptiness with dependent origination, and the dual logic of conventional and ultimate truth.



## Madhyamaka (Nāgārjuna): Dependent Origination, Emptiness, and the Middle Way

Madhyamaka reorients the discourse on emptiness by identifying it with dependent origination: “whatever arises dependently is declared empty; this is dependent designation; it is the middle path.” Nāgārjuna targets svabhāva (inherent existence), demonstrating through context-specific reductios that neither causal relations, nor motion/change, nor personal identity, nor epistemic instruments can be self-grounded. Emptiness is thus the absence of svabhāva—the lack of any ontological bedrock—without succumbing to nihilism. The doctrine of the two truths preserves the efficacy of conventional practices and话语 while situating them as empty of intrinsic reality.[^2][^1]

### 4.1 Emptiness-of-svabhāva

Svabhāva functions as both an ontological and a cognitive target. Ontologically, it names substance or “primary existents” that would ground the world independent of other factors; cognitively, it captures the default superimposition of intrinsic natures onto phenomena. Nāgārjuna’s analysis shows that change, causal efficacy, and ethical responsibility are intelligible only because nothing possesses fixed, intrinsic nature. Emptiness, on this view, is not a positive essence but a corrective to projection: the true nature of phenomena is precisely their lack of inherent nature.[^2]

### 4.2 The Two Truths and Language

Madhyamaka rejects the correspondence theory of truth because there is no mind-independent “ready-made world” to which words could isomorphicly correspond. Instead, truth is analyzed in terms of assertability conditions within conventional practices. Emptiness itself is not an ultimate truth (paramārtha) in the sense of a further thing to be endorsed; it is the intelligible absence of intrinsic nature that disarms reification. In this way, the two truths safeguard both ethical life and the path: conventional truth (samvṛti) remains the pragmatic domain of causes, effects, agents, and texts; ultimate truth (paramārtha) is the absence of svabhāva in all these—emptiness itself included.[^2][^1]

### 4.3 Middle Way beyond Extremes

Avoiding eternalism (substantialism) and nihilism, Nāgārjuna’s Middle Way is a disciplined refusal to endorse any view that would “bottom out” reality. The negative dialectic opens space for compassion and skillful means: since phenomena are empty, they are malleable; since conventions are dependently arisen, they can be improved. There is a deep convergence with early Buddhist ethics: non-attachment and wisdom mutually reinforce, now underwritten by a non-foundational ontology that denies any ultimate kernel for the self or the world.[^2][^1]

To consolidate the argumentative grammar, Table 3 maps representative Madhyamaka refutation patterns across domains.

Table 3. Madhyamaka refutation targets and consequence patterns

| domain | target of refutation | consequence for causation/change/identity |
|---|---|---|
| causation | four modes of cause–effect (from self/other/both/neither) assuming svabhāva | cause/effect mutually dependent; causal relation conceptually constructed; no intrinsic causal identities |
| motion/change | mover/motion as independent existents | property-absence and property-duplication arguments; no fixed mover or motion separable from conceptual design |
| personal identity | substantial self as substratum of properties | agent as illusion; no owner of properties; ethical responsibility preserved conventionally |
| epistemology | epistemic instruments (pramāṇa) and objects (prameya) as self-established | mutual establishment without intrinsic natures; reflective equilibrium replaces foundational proof |

Nāgārjuna’s method is not a single “master argument” but a family of dialectical refutations tailored to contested domains. In each, emptiness and dependent origination reciprocally clarify one another: to be dependently arisen is to be empty; to be empty is to be dependently arisen.[^2][^1]



## Chandrakīrti and the Indian Tradition: Commentaries and the Prajñāpāramitā Link

Chandrakīrti’s Madhyamakāvatāra (“Entrance to the Middle Way”) is among the most influential elaborations of Nāgārjuna’s view, integrating Prajñāpāramitā themes—non-attachment to signs, non-attainment, not relying on proliferations—with a coherent account of the path. In Tibetan transmission, it achieves semi-canonical status as the core training text for Madhyamaka reasoning and the refine­ment of prajñā.[^5][^15][^6][^7][^8]

Chandrakīrti stresses non-affirming negation: emptiness is not a thesis to defend but the failure of any coherent thesis to capture reality on its own terms. He also clarifies the function of the two truths, the status of emptiness as a “pure negation,” and the continuity of Madhyamaka insight with the bodhisattva’s compassionate activity. Tibetan exposures summarize his pith as an uncompromising middle way that integrates view and practice without reifying emptiness into a “thing.”[^5][^15][^6][^8]

## Lotus Sūtra: Buddha-Nature, Skillful Emptiness, and the “One Vehicle”

The Lotus Sūtra (Saddharmapuṇḍarīka) stands as a keystone of East Asian Buddhism, re-visioning the Buddha’s career as eternally enlightened and proclaiming the universal capacity for Buddhahood. Contemporary expositors within the Madhyamaka lineage emphasize a doctrinal movement in which emptiness (śūnyatā) functions as skillful means (upāya) to loosen reification, while buddha-nature (tathāgatagarbha) is taught as the innermost ground of hope and confidence. On this reading, emptiness is preparatory: it unhooks clinging to ego and false absolutes; the final teaching discloses the inherent, inexhaustible purity of beings.[^19][^21][^12]

Table 5 summarizes the doctrinal matrix around buddha-nature, emptiness, and skillful means in the Lotus Sūtra discourse as presented in accessible sources.

Table 5. Lotus Sūtra doctrinal matrix

| axis | teaching | practice/ethics |
|---|---|---|
| buddha-nature | Universal tathāgatagarbha; “base” and “potential” chapters | Confidence in inherent purity; devotions and aspirations disclose inner buddha |
| emptiness (śūnyatā) | Skillful means to counter reification; temporary but necessary | Deconstructs ego-clinging; prepares recognition of buddha-nature |
| one vehicle (ekayāna) | Single path disclosing shared buddha-nature | Compassionate vows; universality replaces exclusivist elites |

The Lotus Sūtra thus reframes emptiness as a doorway rather than a destination: a medicine for attachment that yields to a positive vision of buddhahood already resident in sentient beings. Expositors in the Tibetan world would later debate the ontological status of this positive language, with some (Gelug) reading it as provisional and others (Jonang) as ultimate.[^21][^12]



## Zen Buddhism: Mu, Koans, Satori, and the Practice of Emptiness

Zen inherits Madhyamaka’s desconfirmation of intrinsic natures yet translates it into a praxis-first grammar. Through kōan training and zazen, Zen engineers non-dual breakthroughs that sever the grip of dualistic reasoning. “Mu”—the monosyllabic “no” of Joshu’s “Does a dog have buddha-nature? No!”—does not deny reality; it denies the frame in which the question makes sense. Satori or kenshō names the experiential realization of suchness (tathatā) as a non-discriminatory awareness that saturates ordinary life.[^4]

### 7.1 Satori/Kenshō and “Dropping Off Body-Mind”

Zen describes enlightenment in lived-body terms: Dōgen’s “dropping off the body and the mind” (shinjin datsuraku) signals the collapse of the subject/object split. This is not a trancelike escape but the integra­tion of clarity and compassion in daily activities. In the classical formulation, “practice-realization” (shushō ittai) collapses the means/end duality: just sitting is the activity of buddhahood, not a path to it.[^4]

### 7.2 “Mu” and Experiential Nothingness

Mu condenses “no-thought and no-image” (munen musō), pointing to an experiential “bottomless ground” before the bifurcation of being and non-being. Teachers warn against answering mu with metaphysical assertions or binary logic; one must “kick through the bottom of the bucket” with the whole person. This ground is paradoxically fertile: it is “absolutely nothing” that is not a negation of things but their uninvented condition of arising.[^4]

|---|---|---|

Zen articulates this phenomenological arc through a threefold training: adjust body (posture, diet), adjust breath (counting, rhythm), adjust mind (non-grasping awareness), culminating in “no-mind” (mushin)—not blankness but freedom from egoic elaboration. Table 7 condenses these stages.

| mind adjustment | letting-go of elaboration | Non-mind (mushin); non-dual presence in ordinary activities |

In sum, Zen domesticates emptiness as immediacy: mu and kōan function as instruments of de-conceptualization; zazen embodies the realization that “form is emptiness, emptiness is form” in lived behavior.[^4]



## Daoism: Wu/You, Wu-Wei, Ziran, and Emptiness as Function

Daoism gives “non-being” a positive valence as the inexhaustible source of being. The Daodejing and Zhuangzi deploy paradox and parable to transvalue ordinary striving: the sage, aligned with Dao, “does nothing yet leaves nothing undone.” The function of emptiness is thus practical and ethical: to make space (as in a bowl or a room), to harness responsiveness, and to reduce the friction of egoic intervention.[^3][^10]

### 8.1 Laozi: Dao, Wu/You, and Ziran

The DDJ introduces Dao as nameless and ineffable, the source of differentiation—“Dao gives birth to One; One gives birth to Two; Two gives birth to Three; Three gives birth to the ten thousand things.” Laozi then states a generative axiom: “All things under heaven are born of being; being is born of non-being.” Two classical commentarial frames highlight this polarity. Heshang Gong reads wu as limitlessness, the inexhaustible source of vital qi; Wang Bi reads it as an ontological ground that cannot itself be a being, a conceptual necessity prior to temporal origins. Ziran—“self-so-ing”—names Dao’s way of working and the ethical ideal of spontaneity. Wu-wei is not passivity; it is non-coercive, desire-free action in harmony with Dao’s self-so-ing.[^3]

Two recurring aphorisms make the point: (1) “The Way never acts yet nothing is left undone,” and (2) “The highest virtue is not virtuous.” The first reorients agency toward minimal interference; the second undermines performative moralism in favor of an uncontrived virtue (de) that flows from Dao.[^3][^13]

### 8.2 Zhuangzi: Naturalism, Skepticism, and “Perfect Human”

Zhuangzi’s naturalism dissolves the brittle boundary between “nature” and “convention”: human practices are themselves patterns in nature’s manifold ways. His skepticism is “mild” and constructive: we cannot escape indexical perspectives, but we can practice epistemic modesty and learn from other vantage points. Emptiness here is methodological: “fast the mind,” “forget the baggage of opinions,” and “wander without aim,” allowing spontaneous virtuosity (de) to arise. The parable of Butcher Ding illustrates wu-wei as effortless skill in which the artisan’s action is coextensive with the contours of the task, not with self-assertive will.[^9][^22]

Daoist emptiness is thus a theory of functionality: the “value of emptiness” (DDJ ch. 11) is that a bowl’s usefulness lies in its hollow; the room’s function depends on its void. Emptiness is what affords responsive efficacy. Table 8 sets out the classical interplay.

Table 8. Wu/You in Daodejing and Zhuangzi: interpretive frames

| theme | Laozi (DDJ) | Zhuangzi |
|---|---|---|
| wu (non-being) | Source of being; limitlessness/ontological ground (Heshang Gong/Wang Bi) | Openness to possibilities; freedom from fixed norms |
| you (being) | “Ten thousand things” arise from Dao via differentiation | Thing-kinds are context-dependent; fluid, indexical |
| ziran | Self-so-ing of Dao; ethical spontaneity | Skillful adaptation; virtuoso know-how |
| wu-wei | Non-coercive, effortless action | Flow-state in practice; “following the natural makeup” |

This functional model of emptiness deepens the comparative resonance with Zen: the “bottomless ground” of mu and the Daoist “empty functioning” both highlight responsiveness without reification.[^3][^9][^13][^22]



## Tibetan Buddhism: Bhūmi, Stong Pa Nyid (Voidness), Mahāmudrā, and Dzogchen

Madhyamaka is not merely a school in Tibet; it is the doctrinal backbone across which Tibetan thinkers articulate the subtleties of voidness, the two truths, and the immediacy of realization. Gelug interpreters (after Tsongkhapa) systematize Prasaṅgika Madhyamaka and refine the object of refutation (dgag-bya), while Nyingma and Kagyu currents (often under shentong influences) emphasize the ultimate as empty of adventitious stain but not empty of its own luminous qualities. Mahāmudrā and Dzogchen translate these philosophical stakes into a direct recognition of mind’s empty–luminous nature, often described as gnostic immediacy (rigpa) that is primordially pure.[^17][^16][^12]

### 9.1 Prasaṅgika Refinement and Bhūmi

Tibetan debates focus on how emptiness is known in advanced meditation, and what precisely is refuted. In Gelug analysis, voidness refutes the Rangzhin (self-established) existence of conventional objects. In Nyingma analysis, the objects of conventional designation themselves (tha-snyad-pa) are the primary target of refutation. Consequently, the styles of conceptual meditation on emptiness diverge. Importantly, in non-conceptual absorption (mnyam-bzhag), both traditions converge: there is a shared recognition of the same voidness, despite differing conceptual architectures. On the bhūmi (ground/level) framing, realization of emptiness marks the first of the bodhisattva levels for many Tibetan schemes, but it is not the end of the path; further bhumis deepen compassion, skill, and integration.[^16][^17]

### 9.2 Rangtong vs Shentong

Rangtong (“self-empty”) holds that all phenomena—including buddha-mind—are empty of intrinsic nature; emptiness is a pure negation with no residual positive substratum. Shentong (“other-empty”) maintains that the ultimate is empty of everything other than itself (i.e., of adventitious stains), thus preserving the positive presence of buddha qualities and the luminosity of mind as ultimate. These are not merely terminological squabbles: they color the phenomenology of realization, the rhetoric of devotion, and the interpretative fate of buddha-nature literature. A prominent Kagyu dictum attributed to the Third Karmapa gathers them into a single confidence: “Free of mental activity, it is the Great Seal; free of extremes, it is the Great Middle Way; comprising everything, it is called the Great Perfection—be confident that by knowing one, you realize the meaning of all.”[^12]

### 9.3 Mahāmudrā and Dzogchen

Mahāmudrā (“Great Seal”) and Dzogchen (“Great Perfection”) are gnostic traditions: they seek a direct, non-conceptual realization of mind’s nature as empty, pure, and luminous. Dzogchen insists on a “pure vision” from the outset, with a distinct path structure—pointing-out instructions (ngotrö), cutting through (trekchö), and perfected presence (thögal)—to recognize rigpa as primordially pure awareness. Mahāmudrā, while rooted in tantra, admits non-tantric pathways in some lineages, and unfolds through the four yogas—single-pointedness, non-elaboration, single taste, non-meditation—culminating in effortless buddhahood. Despite differences in metaphysics and method, both hold that emptiness is not a void blocking action but the open clarity from which compassionate responsiveness naturally arises.[^12]

Finally, in the Tibetan two-truths framework, the protean world of convention and the pacifying insight of emptiness interpenetrate without confusion. The theory is not merely ontological; it is a tool for navigating practice, exegesis, and debate.[^17]



## Comparative Synthesis: Axes of Meaning and Strategic Resonances

emptiness/nothingness here reveals four interoperable faces:

- Semantic: śūnya as “zero/void,” historically bridging mathematics and philosophy.
- Ontological: absence of svabhāva; no foundational “thing.”
- Phenomenological: mu as experiential ground before dualisms; Daoist “value of emptiness” in function.
- Meditative/logical: suññatā as a perceptual mode; non-affirming negation as method.

All traditions confront the twin pitfalls of eternalism (reifying an essence) and nihilism (denying the reality of conventions). Daoist wu generates you; Zen mu dislodges the logic that would oppose being and non-being; Madhyamaka’s “emptiness equals dependent origination” blocks both extremes. Tibetan rangtong and shentong disagree on what remains after emptiness is seen, but converge in negating intrinsic existence and in affirming compassion in convention. Lotus Sūtra reframes emptiness as upāya, making room for buddha-nature to shine through as confidence, not as a new essence to cling to.[^11][^2][^14]

| Early Buddhism | Emptiness of self/pertaining to self; meditative emptiness | suññatā cetovimutti; not-self analysis | not if read as mode of perception | liberation via non-attachment; calm abiding |
| Madhyamaka | Emptiness of svabhāva; identity with dependent origination | MMK dialectic; two truths | emptiness ≠ non-existence; conventions function | compassion in convention; skillful means |
| Zen | mu, bottomless ground; practice-realization | zazen; kōan training; shushō ittai | mu is not “nothing” as absence | non-dual responsiveness; everyday compassion |
| Daoism | wu as generative; “value of emptiness” | wu-wei; ziran; DDJ/Zhuangzi parables | non-being is fecund, not null | effortless efficacy; ethical restraint from ego |
| Lotus Sūtra | Emptiness as skillful means; buddha-nature | “One vehicle” soteriology | emptiness neither denies nor affirms essence | universal confidence; vows for all beings |
| Tibetan (Gelug) | Prasaṅgika voidness; rangtong | dialectical analysis; analytical meditation | pure negation guards against reification | precision in refutation; steadfast ethics |
| Tibetan (Nyingma/Kagyu) | shentong ultimate | Mahāmudrā/Dzogchen; pointing-out | ultimate is positive luminosity | devotion, immediacy, spontaneous compassion |

To display the interpretative range, Table 13 contrasts Gelug rangtong and Nyingma shentong as discussed above.

Table 13. Rangtong vs Shentong in Tibetan Madhyamaka

| dimension | Gelug rangtong | Nyingma/Kagyu shentong |
|---|---|---|
| object of refutation | self-established existence of conventionals | conventions themselves as illusions in ultimate purity |
| ultimate predicates | emptiness of all, including itself | empty of other, thus luminous and pure |
| buddha-nature | read as provisional/empty | read as ultimate, unconditioned |
| practice emphasis | analytical meditation on voidness | recognition of pristine awareness (rigpa/mahamudra) |

These contrasts, though real, must not eclipse the shared telos: a life lived from emptiness—non-grasping, responsive, and compassionate—in the very conventions that dependently arise.[^16][^12][^17]



## Implications for Practice and Contemporary Scholarship

- The therapeutic logic of negation. Across traditions, emptiness functions as a scalpel. It cuts through reification—be it the eternal self, the intrinsic thing, or the metaphysical ground—without denying the lived world. Zen’s mu and Daoist wu-wei exhibit a pragmatic refusal to “add” to reality; Madhyamaka’s non-affirming negation refuses to “posit” anything beyond dependent arising. This strategy is therapeutic: by loosening grasping, it reduces the affectively toxic consequences of reification and opens space for responsive care.[^4][^3][^2]

- Emptiness and ethical life. The two-truths doctrine in Tibetan thought, the Lotus Sūtra’s one-vehicle ethic, and Daoist non-coercion all maintain that once emptiness is seen, ethics intensifies rather than diminishes. Conventions—vows, compassion, social harmony—become more rather than less weighty because they are seen as freely chosen patterns of care within emptiness, not as scaffoldings for an ultimately real self.[^17][^21]

- Comparative method. Two cautions guide future work. First, distinguish semantic families (śūnya/śūnyatā; wu/you; mu) rather than assume conceptual synonymy; second, track practice context (meditative, ethical, rhetorical) to avoid flattening distinct functions into a single ontology. A clear example is “absolute nothingness” discourse in some Japanese philosophy: while resonant with mu’s experiential language, it must be carefully distinguished from both Daoist wu and Madhyamaka emptiness to avoid category mistakes.[^4][^3][^2]

- Open questions and agenda. The present synthesis leaves several tasks for future research: (a) precise verse-level citations from MMK for each argument domain (causation, motion, identity), (b) primary source documentation of śūnya/śūnyatā in the Upaniṣads and specified commentaries, (c) canonical anchors for bhūmi schematics in relation to voidness across Tibetan schools, and (d) authoritative editions/translations for key Zen passages (Mumonkan, Shōbōgenzō) to support line-by-line exegesis. Finally, a systematic comparative study of “zero/śūnya/pūrṇa” across Sanskrit, Chinese, and Tibetan philosophical usages would help clarify how mathematical and metaphysical notions coevolved in different circles.[^2][^11][^20]
