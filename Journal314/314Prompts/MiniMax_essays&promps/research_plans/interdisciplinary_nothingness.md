# Nothingness Across Disciplines: An Interdisciplinary Synthesis

## Executive Summary

Across physics, mathematics, psychology, neuroscience, literature, and anthropology, “nothingness” is never merely the absence of something. In physics, even the most empty-seeming vacuum teems with zero-point energy and is described by quantum fields in their ground state; on cosmic scales, vast underdensities—“voids”—form a filamentary web, while a small, positive vacuum energy appears to drive accelerating expansion. Mathematics, similarly, reveals nothing as structured: the empty set ∅ is axiomatically guaranteed, topologically clopen, and foundational for number construction; zero is an identity element, and “null” constructs (e.g., null spaces, null hypotheses) encode information. In mind and culture, nothingness emerges as existential anxiety and the human search for meaning, as neural network reconfigurations (including the default mode network) that accompany ego dissolution and altered states, and as ritualized thresholds—liminal spaces—where transformation is staged through symbolic emptiness. Literature renders the experience of void through absurdist and existential narratives, while Eastern philosophies conceptualize emptiness as dependent origination and generative “wu/mu,” a positive ground of compassion and wisdom rather than negation.

Key convergences and divergences across fields include: the productive, structuring role of “nothing” (mathematical ∅; physical vacuum energy; ritual liminality) versus the threatening or meaningless void (existential anxiety; absurdist narratives). Physics contributes precise empirical constraints but faces deep puzzles (cosmological constant problem). Mathematics offers formal rigor without metaphysical commitment. Psychology and neuroscience track lived experience and its neural substrates but must guard against category errors when linking subjective void to network-level mechanisms. Anthropology and comparative philosophy illuminate cultural practices and concepts of emptiness that guide transformation without reducing them to purely psychological terms.

Strategic implications are far-reaching. Cultivating healthy relationships with “emptiness” can inform clinical care (e.g., working with ego dissolution in controlled contexts), contemplative pedagogy (e.g., training non-fixated awareness), ethical leadership (e.g., embracing uncertainty rather than defaulting to authority), and scientific research programs (e.g., integrating phenomenology with network neuroscience). Future work should prioritize interdisciplinary dialogue that respects methodological differences, triangulates findings, and resists sloppy analogies. In particular, careful bridges are needed between the physical vacuum and the experiential void—bridges that can be constructive without conflating distinct domains of inquiry.[^1][^2][^23]



## Orientation and Framework

Defining “nothingness” across disciplines requires distinguishing among at least four families of meaning:

- Physical nothing: spaces with few or no particles (classical vacuum), quantum fields in their ground state (quantum vacuum), large-scale underdensities (cosmic voids), and the cosmological constant or dark energy that contributes to spacetime dynamics.
- Logical-mathematical nothing: the empty set (∅), zero as an identity element, null structures that carry information (e.g., kernels), and topological empty spaces.
- Experiential void: existential anxiety, meaninglessness, boredom, and the uncanniness of contingency; altered states in which ego boundaries attenuate or dissolve.
- Ritual liminality: threshold phases in rites of passage that are structurally “betwixt and between,” marked by communitas and anti-structure.

Nothingness in these domains is never purely absence. In physics, zero-point energy and quantum fluctuations make the vacuum active; in mathematics, “nothing” grounds structures; in psychology and anthropology, liminal or “empty” phases enable transformation; and in existential philosophy and literature, confronting emptiness can catalyze freedom, authenticity, and ethical responsibility. The cross-disciplinary strategy here synthesizes conceptual analysis with empirical findings, juxtaposing insights to illuminate productive roles and pitfalls of “nothing” talk, while explicitly avoiding category errors.

Methodologically, the report proceeds in three moves. First, it grounds each domain with state-of-the-art knowledge and key anomalies. Second, it tracks convergences—ways that “void” enables structure, meaning, and transformation—alongside critical divergences in what counts as evidence and explanation. Third, it distills strategic implications for science and society, focusing on where interdisciplinary dialogue can add value without erasing disciplinary specificity.[^23]

## Physics of “Nothing”

Modern physics has transformed the idea of emptiness from a classical absence into a set of rich, counterintuitive structures. On the smallest scales, quantum fields构成 reality’s fabric; on the largest, underdensities and horizons define the universe’s architecture; and a tiny, positive vacuum energy appears to shape cosmic destiny. “Nothing” in physics is thus active, structured, and sometimes unstable.[^1][^2][^36]

### Quantum Vacuum and Zero-Point Energy

In quantum field theory (QFT), the vacuum is the state of lowest energy. But that state is not inert. Fields are analogous to arrays of quantum harmonic oscillators; even at zero temperature, uncertainty implies jitter, yielding zero-point energy and fluctuations. The vacuum is therefore a seething, minimum-energy substrate in which particles are excitations.[^5][^1]

This picture is not merely speculative. The Casimir effect—an attraction between uncharged conducting plates in a vacuum—demonstrably arises from modifications of vacuum fluctuations, with force scaling as the inverse fourth power of plate separation. First predicted in the mid-twentieth century, it was experimentally verified in the late 1990s and has since become a canonical proof that “empty” space is physically active.[^2][^37] Even more strikingly, physicists have recently exhibited controlled processes that extract energy from the vacuum’s quantum fluctuations under carefully engineered conditions, further underscoring that QFT’s “nothing” is a resource, not a null set.[^4]

Vacuum structure extends beyond energy to stability. Some scalar fields (such as the Higgs) may rest in metastable configurations. If our universe inhabits a “false vacuum,” a bubble could nucleate and expand at near light speed, converting space into a lower-energy “true vacuum” and thereby “rewriting physics.” Alternatively, certain extra-dimensional setups allow an instability—“bubble of nothing”—in which spacetime itself ceases to exist, with a bubble of complete emptiness propagating outward. Though sensational, such scenarios are consistent with the rules of quantum field theory and gravity, and contemporary work explores how robust such instabilities might be under broad assumptions.[^1][^2]

The upshot is that “nothing” in QFT is a structured, dynamic entity. It possesses energy, tolerates multiple metastable states, and can host phase transitions that alter the laws of physics—sometimes radically.

### Dark Energy and the Cosmological Constant

The universe’s large-scale dynamics provide another window on “empty.” Observations of distant supernovae and the cosmic microwave background indicate that space’s expansion is accelerating. A leading interpretation is that vacuum energy—formalized as Einstein’s cosmological constant (Λ)—has a small, positive value, acting as “antigravity” on cosmic scales.[^6][^9]

This idea raises a profound puzzle. QFT calculations of vacuum energy, when naively combined with gravity, predict contributions orders of magnitude larger than the observed value—often expressed as a discrepancy on the order of 10^120 between the Planck scale and the observed cosmological energy density. The mismatch is so extreme that it is often called the worst prediction in all of physics.[^7][^2] Conceptually, the issue is not merely quantitative: it exposes the difficulty of marrying quantum field theory with general relativity at energy scales that would make vacuum energy gravitate with the observed tiny magnitude. Current proposals span anthropic explanations, new symmetries, modified gravity, and emergent or dynamical dark energy; yet none has closed the gap decisively. The problem persists as a signpost that our understanding of “nothing” in gravity is incomplete.[^6][^7][^8]

### Cosmic Voids and Large-Scale Structure

On grand scales, the universe exhibits a web-like architecture: filaments of galaxies and dark matter channel matter into clusters and walls, while vast underdensities—voids—stretch between them. These voids are not entirely empty; they contain diffuse gas and dark matter, but they are markedly galaxy-poor. Some “supervoids” extend hundreds of millions of light-years and have been posited to explain cold spots or other anomalies in the cosmic microwave background, though the jury is still out on specific claims.[^41][^38][^40][^42][^44]

Void evolution is shaped by gravitational instability and tides across large scales. Simulations and theory indicate that voids are dynamic: they expand, intersect, and leave imprints on galaxy flows. Their internal structure is rich, with subvoids, walls, and filaments—features that make voids more than “holes in the web.” They are, in effect, laboratories for understanding structure formation and the role of dark energy in shaping underdensities.[^39][^38]

### Cosmological Horizons

Nothingness also enters through limits to observation. Particle horizons bound the region from which light has had time to reach us; event horizons mark surfaces beyond which events cannot affect a given observer (e.g., around black holes or in de Sitter-like spacetimes). Horizons do not imply that beyond them there is “nothing,” only that information cannot cross certain boundaries. The interplay of vacuum energy, horizons, and spacetime structure therefore shapes what observers can see and when—rendering “nothing” an epistemic artifact of finite causal reach rather than a statement about ontological emptiness.[^36]

In sum, physics reframes “nothing” as structured dynamism (quantum vacuum), a modest but cosmically consequential energy density (dark energy), and as vast, evolving architectures (voids), while horizons remind us that some “emptiness” is a matter of vantage, not essence.[^1][^2][^38]



## Mathematics of “Nothing”

Mathematics does not treat “nothing” as an undifferentiated absence. Instead, it has crafted a suite of precise objects—empty set, zero, null spaces, vacuous truth—that are productive, foundational, and surprisingly informative. Nothing becomes a scaffolding for constructing and analyzing structures.[^24][^26][^25][^27]

### Empty Set Theory and Set-Theoretic Foundations

The empty set ∅ is the unique set with no elements. Its existence is guaranteed by an axiom of set theory, and it is a subset of every set. In topology, ∅ is both open and closed (clopen), and it is compact; this duality is not a curiosity but part of the axioms that define open sets. For constructions of number, the empty set is foundational: in the von Neumann representation, 0 is defined as ∅, 1 as {∅}, 2 as {∅, {∅}}, and so on, building the naturals inductively. The empty set thus sits at the very origin of number.[^24][^26][^25]

Crucially, ∅ must be distinguished from {∅}. The former has no members; the latter has one member—the empty set. In axiomatic set theory, such distinctions prevent paradoxes, secure induction, and underwrite a coherent hierarchy of infinities. Philosophically, mathematics here treats “nothing” as a bona fide object that participates in structural relations. This is not a metaphysical claim about reality; it is a discipline-relative stance that “nothing” can be used coherently and fruitfully as a primitive.[^24][^26][^25]

### Logic, Negation, and Nullity

Logical negation allows propositions to be false, and the falsum (⊥) expresses contradiction. Yet formal systems also deploy “vacuous truth”: statements that are true simply because there are no counterexamples to consider. For instance, “all elements of ∅ satisfy P(x)” is true for any P, because ∅ has no elements to violate it. Such vacuity is not trivial; it underwrites quantifier transformations, proofs by exhaustion, and defaults in specifications.

“Null” constructs likewise encode structure. In linear algebra, the null space (kernel) of a transformation consists of all vectors mapped to the zero vector. The dimension of the null space (nullity) is not a statement of absence but of constraint: it tells us about dependencies among columns and the rank of a map. Rank–nullity theorems quantify how domain dimensions split between meaningful signal and degeneracy, showing that “zero” here is diagnostic, not merely negative.[^26]

### Topology and Empty Spaces

Topologically, the empty set is included as an open set by definition. As a result, ∅ is clopen: its complement (the whole space) is also open, and ∅ is its own boundary in a precise sense. This duality supports disconnection and compactness arguments and appears in proofs throughout analysis and geometry. The “emptiness” of ∅, in this context, is a structural asset that enables clean separation properties and clarifies the logic of nearness and continuity.[^27]

## Psychology of Nothingness

If mathematics and physics treat nothingness as structure, psychology engages it as experienced contingency and limit: the anxiety of freedom, the nausea of absurdity, the numbness of anomie. Thoughtfully engaged, existential “void” can be a catalyst for meaning-making; mishandled, it can escalate into despair. Clinical care thus requires boundaries that respect the transforming potential of emptiness without exploiting it.[^23][^28][^29]

### Existential Anxiety and the Void

Existential anxiety arises when we confront fundamental questions about mortality, freedom, responsibility, and meaning. It differs from generalized anxiety in its depth and specificity: it is the affect that accompanies the recognition of contingency—how things could be otherwise, including ourselves. Classic existential analysis emphasizes “world-shattering moods” (anxiety, boredom, nausea) as signals that call us beyond conformity (“the They”) toward authenticity: owning our freedom, acknowledging our facticity, and taking responsibility for the values we enact. Properly held, these moods do not flatten meaning; they reveal it as made, not given.[^23]

Therapeutically, clinicians differentiate existential void from clinical depression. The former may respond to meaning-oriented interventions; the latter requires evidence-based treatments (e.g., psychotherapy and, when appropriate, medication) and risk assessment. Skillful work blends psychoeducation (normalizing the encounter with contingency) with practices that anchor agency (e.g., values clarification, commitment to action), while avoiding intellectualization that sidesteps responsibility.

### Depressive Emptiness and Anhedonia

Depressive emptiness is often marked by anhedonia—the loss of pleasure or interest—and a narrowing of agency. Here, “emptiness” is not a philosophical disclosure but a symptom constellation. While some interventions intentionally bring emptiness “into awareness,” clinical practice must be vigilant: eliciting existential material without adequate containment can destabilize. Risk management and stepped care (from supportive psychotherapy to specialized modalities) are essential. Existential insights are complements, not substitutes, for evidence-based clinical protocols.[^29]

### Mystical Experiences and Ego Dissolution

Ego dissolution—a减弱ed or dissolved sense of self—occurs in contemplative practice and in psychedelic states. Psychedelic research suggests that changes in selfhood correlate with alterations in large-scale brain networks, notably decreases in the integrity of the default mode network (DMN) and changes in salience network (SLN) dynamics. Such states can be beneficial in controlled contexts, but they are not panaceas; set, setting, and integration matter. Clinicians should be aware that “self-transcendence” can coexist with vulnerability (e.g., during the “come-down”), and must be prepared to titrate exposure and support integration.[^10][^11][^13][^14]

### Jungian Shadow and Unconscious “Void”

In Jungian psychology, the shadow comprises repressed or disowned aspects of the personality—both adaptive and problematic. Bringing the shadow into conscious awareness can feel like encountering an internal void: a disowned otherness within. This work aims at integration rather than eradication; the goal is to expand the ego’s sphere of responsibility and compassion, integrating qualities that were previously split. In this frame, “emptiness” is relational—space for the other within—rather than a vacuum to be filled.[^28]

## Neuroscience of Nothingness

Neuroscience investigates how experiences of void or self-dissolution map onto brain networks and dynamics. Two large-scale systems—the default mode network (DMN) and the salience network (SLN)—feature prominently in this literature, alongside midline structures and medial temporal lobe circuits.[^12][^13][^10]

### Default Mode Network and Self-Referential Processing

The default mode network, anchored by the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC), medial prefrontal cortex (MPFC), and inferior parietal lobule (IPL), is associated with narrative self-referential processing, autobiographical memory, and mental time travel. DMN activity typically decreases during externally focused tasks and increases during rest and mind-wandering. Alterations in DMN integrity are correlated with changes in self-related cognition, including rumination and autobiographical salience.[^12][^13]

In meditation research, experienced practitioners often show reduced DMN activity in particular states, alongside changes in functional connectivity among executive and attentional networks. Longitudinal work indicates that meditation can reconfigre central executive and DMN systems, consistent with training in attentional control and reduced self-referential reactivity.[^14]

### Ego Dissolution: Mechanisms and Networks

Psychedelic states provide a powerful window into the neural correlates of selfhood. Across classic hallucinogens (psilocybin, LSD, DMT), studies report decreased integrity and activity in the DMN, altered connectivity with medial temporal lobe structures (including parahippocampal cortex), and changes in SLN activity. This reconfiguration coincides with phenomenology: weakened narrative self, altered body boundaries, and (often) a sense of unity or oceanic consciousness. Predictive processing accounts propose that ego dissolution reflects degraded “binding” across hierarchical models that normally sustain a unified self-model; the brain’s predictive machinery, less constrained by a dominant self-narrative, generates a fluid, decentered mode of awareness.[^10][^11][^13][^15]

These mechanistic accounts do not reduce the richness of mystical experience to network diagrams; rather, they offer testable hypotheses about how representational integration supports selfhood. Importantly, network-level changes are correlates, not explanations; careful design and replication are needed to map specific mechanisms to particular aspects of the ego-dissolution phenotype.

## Literature and the Void

Literature explores nothingness as lived absurdity, silence, and the failure of meaning to cohere. It frequently stages the collapse of linguistic communication and the shock of contingency. Yet even when it depicts futility, it rarely stops at nihilism; often it gestures toward freedom, responsibility, or revolt.

### Absurdist Traditions (Camus, Sartre)

Absurdist fiction and the Theatre of the Absurd emerged in mid-twentieth-century Europe, shaped by war, disillusionment, and the decline of religious narratives. The genre highlights the inability to find inherent purpose, relying on dark humor, incongruity, and the breakdown of communication. Samuel Beckett’s Waiting for Godot is emblematic: characters wait for someone who never arrives, and the play tests the boundary between speech and silence, meaning and vacuity. Martin Esslin’s framing of the “Theatre of the Absurd” underscores a dramaturgy that subverts linear causality and closes on ambiguity rather than resolution.[^17]

Jean-Paul Sartre and Albert Camus are often read together yet diverge in key ways. Camusthemself emphasized the absurd—the clash between human longing for meaning and a silent, indifferent world—arguing for lucidity and revolt without appeal to metaphysics. Sartre, by contrast, developed an existentialist framework in which “existence precedes essence”: humans are “nothing” at first, becoming something through choices that carry responsibility. Sartre’s plays and fiction render the anguish of freedom and the temptations of “bad faith”—denying one’s freedom by conforming to external roles. The two traditions thus treat nothingness differently: Camus as a condition to be faced with clear-eyed defiance, Sartre as a call to create meaning through commitment.[^18][^19][^21]

### Existentialist Fiction

Existentialist novels and stories—whether by Dostoevsky, Kafka, or later writers—stage the crisis of meaning, the arbitrariness of social norms, and the individual's confrontation with contingency. Kafka’s restrained prose and nightmarish bureaucracies expose the absurdity of systems that reify meaning and crush individuality; Sartre’s fiction and drama render the vertigo of radical freedom and the moral demand to avoid self-deception. Across these works, void is less an ontological claim than an occasion for ethical choice: once traditional meanings are stripped away, what do we owe to each other?[^22][^23]

### Eastern Mystical Poetry

Eastern traditions invert nihilism by reframing emptiness as generative. Buddhist śūnyatā denies inherent existence (svabhāva) and locates meaning in dependent origination; form and emptiness are inseparable. Taoist wu and Zen mu name a creative void, a fertile “no-thing” from which beings arise and to which they return; practice (zazen, koans,wu-wei) cultivates non-fixated awareness that is both empty and compassionate. Poets and painters across East Asia encode “negative space” (ma) as an aesthetic principle, revealing the moon by pointing away from the finger. In this constellation, emptiness is not negation but openness—an enabling absence that permits presence to appear.[^23]

## Anthropology of Emptiness

Anthropology reframes “nothingness” as social structure and ritual technique. Liminality—the threshold phase in rites of passage—creates an “anti-structure” where ordinary categories are suspended, allowing for transformation. Shamanic practices intentionally induce “gaps in awareness,” traversing a lived void to reweave relations among persons, places, and powers.

### Rituals of Emptiness and Liminal Spaces

Victor Turner formalized liminality as the ambiguous middle stage in rites of passage, where participants are “neither here nor there.” In this space, ordinary statuses dissolve; hierarchies invert; and communitas emerges—an egalitarian bond grounded in shared vulnerability. Liminality is not a vacuum but a fertile chaos: rituals perform emptiness to generate new social configurations and meanings.[^30][^31][^33]

Contemporary analyses of grief and bereavement show how liminal spaces help societies hold ambiguity and transition. Thin spaces around impending death, hospices, and memorials serve as structured voids where attachment and identity can be reconfigured. Anthropological reflection thus treats emptiness as socially necessary—an enabling interval.[^32][^33]

### Shamanic Journeys and “Void” States

Shamanic traditions cultivated techniques for entering altered states—trance, journeying, and vision—aimed at re-establishing a sense of unity beyond ordinary body-identification. These practices assume that gaps in awareness can be opened intentionally (via drumming, dance, chant, posture, or plant allies) to perceive relations that consensus reality obscures. In such frames, “void” is not the enemy but a passage: a space between forms where healing, knowledge, and relation are brokered.[^34][^35]

Critics note risks: pathologizing trance, cultural appropriation, and the dangers of uncontained altered states. Defenders stress the epistemic humility involved—learning to navigate liminality with guidance, reciprocity, and responsibility to community. Either way, shamanic accounts demonstrate that “emptiness” can be ritual technology rather than mere absence.[^35][^34]

## Cross-Disciplinary Synthesis

Nothingness plays two recurring roles across domains. As structured absence, it enables: physics’ vacuum energy is active; mathematics’ ∅ scaffolds numbers and topology; anthropology’s liminality enables transformation. As existential threat, it destabilizes: anxiety and absurdity confront humans with contingency; psychedelic ego dissolution can disorient; “bubble of nothing” is a (remote) physical instability. Eastern philosophies mediate these poles by reframing emptiness as dependent origination and creative opennes—an enabling absence.

Methodologically, each discipline holds different standards of evidence and inference. Physics relies on quantitative models tested by experiment and observation (Casimir effect; supernova cosmology); mathematics demonstrates theorems within axiomatic systems; psychology and neuroscience combine phenomenological reports with correlational and experimental designs; literature and anthropology craft narrative and interpretive accounts. These differences are strengths: they allow triangulation. They also caution against category errors—most notably, the temptation to treat experiential void as identical to physical vacuum or mathematical ∅.

A minimal set of analogies that cross borders responsibly include: structure-from-void (mathematics: ∅ as scaffold; physics: vacuum fluctuations; anthropology: liminality as anti-structure); generative emptiness (Taoist/Zen wu/mu as creative openness); and binding/unbinding in self-models (neuroscience: DMN integrity; psychology: decentering). Each analogy is limited but productive for generating questions and practices.

## Strategic Implications and Applications

If nothingness can be structured and generative, how might individuals and institutions cultivate healthy relationships with emptiness?

Clinical practice. Psychedelic-assisted therapies and certain contemplative modalities can support ego dissolution and decentering in controlled, supportive contexts. Clinicians should ensure rigorous screening, set-and-setting, dosing, and integration practices, and recognize contraindications. For existential anxiety, integrate meaning-focused psychotherapy (e.g., logotherapy elements) with skills that anchor agency and values. Shadow integration should proceed with attention to safety and pacing.[^10][^14][^28]

Contemplative practice. Training in non-fixated awareness—mindfulness, zazen, koan study—can foster comfort with emptiness without collapsing into dissociation. Pedagogical design should balance openness with ethical scaffolding, emphasizing interdependence rather than detachment.[^23]

Research priorities. Three directions are especially promising. First, triangulating phenomenology and neural mechanisms in altered states: combine first-person reports with convergent measures (fMRI, EEG) and computational modeling. Second, modeling vacuum stability and cosmological dynamics across theories, using cross-validation with astronomical data. Third, mapping liminality in clinical and social contexts (e.g., grief, rites of passage) with mixed methods to identify mechanisms of resilience.[^1][^2][^32]

Technology and ethics. Digital platforms that intentionally create “gaps” (e.g., notification-free intervals, reflective spaces) can support attention and meaning-making. Conversely, designs that flood awareness with stimuli risk amplifying existential anxiety. Organizations can adopt “liminal ethics”—clear transitions between roles and statuses, spaces for dissent and reflection, and rituals that acknowledge change.

## Conclusion

Nothingness, understood across disciplines, is neither monolith nor mirage. In physics, the quantum vacuum and cosmic voids reveal an active substrate; in mathematics, the empty set and zero found entire conceptual systems; in psychology and neuroscience, emptiness, anxiety, and ego dissolution point to the malleability of self-models; in literature and anthropology, void becomes a stage for freedom, transformation, and ethical responsibility.

A strategic reframing follows. Rather than asking whether “nothing” exists, we can ask how structured absences enable phenomena: How do vacuum fluctuations shape particle interactions? How does ∅ make numbers possible? How do liminal spaces remake social bonds? How do decentered states inform care? These questions keep disciplines in conversation without flattening their differences.

Limitations and future work remain. The cosmological constant problem continues to resist a satisfying synthesis; translating neural correlates of ego dissolution into mechanistic explanations requires better designs and replications; anthropological accounts of liminality would benefit from broader comparative datasets; and psychological research must better distinguish existential void from depressive emptiness. Finally, literary and philosophical treatments of “nothingness” demand continued interpretive engagement to resist sensational simplifications.

The most constructive path forward is interdisciplinary: respectful, evidence-seeking, and unafraid of productive ambiguity. If “nothing” has taught us anything, it is that absence can be fertile—if we learn to work with it.[^1][^23]

- Direct, citable physics coverage of cosmological horizons beyond the provided survey is limited here; readers should consult specialized reviews for extended horizon calculus and causal structure.
- The vacuum-energy equivalence with the cosmological constant is discussed at an explanatory level; precision claims warrant consultation of the primary literature and authoritative reviews.
- Psychology’s literature on existential void would benefit from fuller clinical citation; this synthesis emphasizes conceptual clarity over exhaustiveness.
- Neuroscience links between default mode network alterations and the subjective experience of “nothingness/void” remain correlational; causal claims should be framed cautiously.
- Eastern mystical poetry would be strengthened with primary textual citations (e.g., Heart Sūtra, Tao Te Ching), which are beyond the scope of the present synthesis.
- Anthropological primary ethnographies are underrepresented here; future work should integrate more site-specific case studies.
