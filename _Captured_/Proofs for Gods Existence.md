The Argument from Motion by Aquinas
 • Premise 1: Our senses can perceive motion by seeing that things act on one another.
 • Premise 2: Whatever moves is moved by something else.
 • Premise 3: There cannot be an infinite regress of movers.
 • Conclusion: Therefore, there must be a First Mover that creates this chain reaction of motions. This is God.
The Argument from Efficient Cause by Aquinas
 • Premise 1: Everything must have a cause or something that creates an effect on another thing.
 • Premise 2: Nothing can cause itself.
 • Premise 3: There cannot be an infinite regress of causes.
 • Conclusion: Therefore, there must be a First Cause that initiates this chain of causes and effects. This is God.
The Argument from Necessary Being by Aquinas
 • Premise 1: Objects in the world come into existence and pass out of it, so it is possible for them to exist or not exist at any particular time.
 • Premise 2: Nothing can come from nothing.
 • Premise 3: There cannot be an infinite regress of contingent beings.
 • Conclusion: Therefore, there must be a Necessary Being that exists at all times and does not depend on anything else for its existence. This is God.
The Argument from Gradation by Aquinas
 • Premise 1: There are different degrees of goodness in different things.
 • Premise 2: There is a standard of goodness by which we can compare and rank things.
 • Premise 3: The standard of goodness must be the most perfect being, otherwise there would be something more good than it.
 • Conclusion: Therefore, there must be a being of the highest form of good. This perfect being is God.
The Argument from Design by Aquinas
 • Premise 1: All things have an order or arrangement that leads them to a particular goal.
 • Premise 2: The order of the universe cannot be the result of chance, as it is too complex and purposeful.
 • Premise 3: Design and purpose imply intelligence on the part of the designer.
 • Conclusion: Therefore, there must be a divine intelligence that designed and ordered the universe. This is God.
Pascal’s Wager

## Proof from Linguistic Limitations

1. **Premise 1**: Language is a human construct developed to describe and communicate experiences and phenomena within our universe.
2. **Premise 2**: There exist concepts and experiences that are ineffable, escaping full encapsulation by language.
3. **Premise 3**: The ineffable experiences resonate with a sense of 'Transcendent Reality' that cannot be fully described but can be sensed.
4. **Conclusion**: The limitations of language in capturing these 'Transcendent Realities' point to an entity beyond human construct and understanding. This entity is what we refer to as God.

## Proof from Existential Desolation

1. **Premise 1**: Existential dread and desolation are universal human experiences.
2. **Premise 2**: These states often lead to profound transformations and revelations, experienced as 'touching the Divine'.
3. **Premise 3**: Nihilism and existential dread are, therefore, not merely psychological states but are indicative of a 'yearning' for the Transcendent.
4. **Conclusion**: The transformative power of existential dread suggests a transcendent reality or being that instigates this transformation. This is God.

## The Quantum Cosmological Argument

1. **Premise 1**: In quantum mechanics, particles can exist in a superposition, occupying multiple states at once.
2. **Premise 2**: The act of observation collapses this superposition into a single state.
3. **Premise 3**: Before observation, the particles exist in all possible states simultaneously, implying an 'infinite possibility'.
4. **Conclusion**: The existence of 'infinite possibility' until collapsed by observation suggests a First Observer who sets the parameters of existence. This is God.

## Proof from Nihiltheistic Symbiosis

1. **Premise 1**: Nihilism argues that life lacks inherent meaning, while theism argues for a higher purpose.
2. **Premise 2**: These worldviews are generally considered mutually exclusive.
3. **Premise 3**: The persistence and co-existence of both views in human thought and experience suggest they are two sides of the same coin, hinting at a more complex Transcendent Reality.
4. **Conclusion**: The symbiotic relationship between Nihilism and Theism indicates a unifying Transcendent Entity that encompasses both. This is God.

## The Argument from Information Complexity

1. **Premise 1**: The universe contains systems (e.g., DNA, quantum mechanics) that are highly complex and rich in information.
2. **Premise 2**: Information complexity at this scale and intricacy cannot arise from random processes alone; it requires a guiding mechanism.
3. **Premise 3**: No known natural mechanism can account for the highest complexities of information we observe.
4. **Conclusion**: Therefore, a transcendent source of information complexity must exist. This source is God.

## The Argument from Meta-Ethical Necessity

1. **Premise 1**: Moral values are experienced as obligations, not just preferences.
2. **Premise 2**: Obligations imply an obligor, a source that sets these obligations.
3. **Premise 3**: The nature of these obligations is such that they cannot be fully accounted for by social constructs or evolutionary advantages.
4. **Conclusion**: Therefore, a transcendent obligor must exist that grounds these meta-ethical necessities. This is God.

## The Argument from Unified Diversity

1. **Premise 1**: The universe displays an astonishing variety of forms, phenomena, and experiences.
2. **Premise 2**: Despite this diversity, there is a remarkable consistency in the laws of physics, hinting at a unified underlying principle.
3. **Premise 3**: This unified diversity cannot be a mere product of chance, given its complexity and the fine-tuning required for the existence of life.
4. **Conclusion**: Therefore, a transcendent principle must exist that unifies this diversity. This is God.

1. **Premise 1**: Humans have a capacity for profound aesthetic experiences that often elicit a sense of the sublime or the transcendent.
2. **Premise 2**: These experiences are not easily reducible to mere biochemical reactions or evolutionary advantages.
3. **Premise 3**: Such experiences often push individuals toward a quest for higher understanding or meaning.
4. **Conclusion**: Therefore, a transcendent source must exist that gives rise to these profound aesthetic experiences. This is God.
