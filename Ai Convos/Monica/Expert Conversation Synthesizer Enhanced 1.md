          <DEFINITION>

          </DEFINITION>
          <IMPLEMENTATION>

          </IMPLEMENTATION>

          </RATIONALE>

        <PRINCIPLE_2>
          <NAME>Scaffolding</NAME>
          <DEFINITION>

          </DEFINITION>

            - Intermediate scaffolding: Fewer examples, more open-ended problems, partial procedures
            - Advanced scaffolding: Minimal support, open-ended inquiry, independent problem-solving
            
            The goal is to make scaffolding invisible-learners don't notice it's there, but they couldn't succeed without it.
          </IMPLEMENTATION>
          <RATIONALE>
            Vygotsky's "zone of proximal development" shows that learners can accomplish more with support than without it. Effective scaffolding enables learners to reach beyond their current capabilities, gradually developing new competencies.
          </RATIONALE>
        </PRINCIPLE_2>

        <PRINCIPLE_3>
          <NAME>Active Learning</NAME>
          <DEFINITION>
            Engage learners in problem-solving, decision-making, and application of knowledge. Rather than passively receiving information, learners actively construct understanding through engagement.
          </DEFINITION>
          <IMPLEMENTATION>

            - Pose open-ended questions that prompt reflection
            - Provide scenarios requiring application of concepts
            - Enable learners to test their understanding
            - Create feedback loops enabling course correction
          </IMPLEMENTATION>
          <RATIONALE>
            Research shows that active learning produces deeper understanding and better retention than passive reception. Learners who engage actively develop not just knowledge but also skills and dispositions.
          </RATIONALE>
        </PRINCIPLE_3>

    <MISCONCEPTION_001>
      <MISCONCEPTION_CLAIM>
        "Synthesis is just summarization-condensing the chat into shorter form while preserving all information."
      </MISCONCEPTION_CLAIM>
      <WHY_IT_SEEMS_TRUE>

        - Both reduce volume of material
        - Both aim to make material more accessible
      </WHY_IT_SEEMS_TRUE>
      <CORRECTION>
        [STATED] Synthesis differs fundamentally from summarization:
        - Summarization: Reduces volume while preserving content (lossy compression)
        - Synthesis: Reveals relationships and creates new understanding (generative process)
        
        Synthesis actually INCREASES volume (through elaboration) while INCREASING clarity (through relationship mapping). The goal is not to reduce but to integrate and clarify.
      </CORRECTION>
      <HOW_TO_AVOID>
        - Understand that synthesis is generative, not reductive
        - Look for new insights emerging from synthesis, not just condensed versions of original
        - Evaluate synthesis by whether it enables new understanding, not just whether it's shorter
        - Recognize that good synthesis may be longer than original material
      </HOW_TO_AVOID>
    </MISCONCEPTION_001>

      <MISCONCEPTION_CLAIM>
        "The goal is to achieve 100% saturation in a single pass; multiple cycles are inefficient."
      </MISCONCEPTION_CLAIM>
      <WHY_IT_SEEMS_TRUE>
        - Seems more efficient to do everything at once
        - Multiple cycles seem redundant
        - Iterative processes can appear wasteful
      </WHY_IT_SEEMS_TRUE>
      <CORRECTION>
        [STATED] Iterative Densification Process is fundamentally different from single-pass approach:
        - Single pass: Misses hidden relationships; produces incomplete synthesis; fails to achieve true saturation
        - Multiple cycles: Each cycle reveals new layers; produces increasingly sophisticated understanding; achieves genuine saturation
        
        [INFERRED] Saturation is not a state that can be achieved in one pass; it's a process of progressive deepening. Each cycle reveals what the previous cycle missed, enabling more complete understanding.
      </CORRECTION>
      <HOW_TO_AVOID>
        - Understand that complexity requires multiple passes
        - Recognize that each cycle reveals new material, not redundant material
        - Evaluate cycles by novelty produced, not just effort expended
        - Commit to continuing cycles until saturation is truly achieved
      </HOW_TO_AVOID>

        - Use [INFERRED] label to enable creative interpretation while maintaining transparency
        - Distinguish between creative interpretation and unsupported speculation

        - Maintain density by removing redundancy while preserving completeness
