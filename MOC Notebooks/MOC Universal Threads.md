## Description
**Nihiltheism** is a philosophy that embraces a traditional view of nihilism, asserting that the essential Nothingness of existence is a construct, and possibly the embodiment of the Transcendent or Divine. This philosophy invites us not merely to understand existential despair, but to deeply feel and engage with it. Nihiltheism advocates for an active, ascetic confrontation with Nothingness in our physical world, serving as a constant "reminder" of the Transcendent and fostering a renunciative approach to worldly attachments.

This philosophy intersects with varied philosophical and religious traditions, encompassing existentialists like Søren Kierkegaard and Martin Heidegger, Christian mystics such as Meister Eckhart and Miguel de Molinos, and Eastern philosophies including Buddhism, Vedanta, and Taoism. Thinkers like Emil Cioran and Paul Tillich also find resonance within the Nihiltheistic framework.

Distinct from philosophies that seek to evade existential despair, Nihiltheism encourages immersion within it, though it acknowledges the difficulty of sustaining this state indefinitely. This is evident in the works of many philosophical and religious figures: William James' "divided-self," Kierkegaard’s "double-mindedness," Heidegger’s "always falling" into the they, and Plato's disdain for the human body as it diverts from The Forms.

Nihiltheism queries whether our experience of Nihilism might transcend scientific explanation to denote something Divine. Psychedelics play a notable role within Nihiltheism, as they can evoke Transcendent experiences aligned with core principles such as ego-death and total renunciation of the human condition.

Living as a Nihiltheist involves constant awareness of life's fleeting nature and the physical world's insignificance. Morality within Nihiltheism is viewed through a lens of "non-movement," akin to pacifism or the Taoist philosophy of non-engagement, often interpreted as "doing nothing." In its extreme form, it advocates for moral inaction—not to "do good" or "avoid evil," but as part of the renunciation of the physical world.
